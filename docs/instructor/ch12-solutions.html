Chapter 12: Bose-Einstein Condensation — Solutions

Problem 1: MB High-Temperature Ground State Fraction (Q1)

Answer: (b) $1/2$.

From Eq. (7), $\langle N_\text{ground}^\text{MB}\rangle = \frac{N}{e^{-\beta\epsilon} + 1}$. At high temperature, $\beta\epsilon \to 0$, so $e^{-\beta\epsilon} \to 1$ and $\langle N_\text{ground}^\text{MB}\rangle \to N/2$. With two states, each gets half the particles classically.

Problem 2: Why Only the Ground State Condenses (Q2)

Answer: (b).

For bosons, $\mu < \epsilon_0 = 0$. For any excited state $i$ with $\epsilon_i > 0$, the gap $\epsilon_i - \mu > \epsilon_i > 0$ never closes, so $\langle N_i \rangle = \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon_i - \mu)} - 1}$ remains bounded. Only for the ground state does $\epsilon_0 - \mu = -\mu \to 0^+$ as $\mu \to 0^-$, allowing $\langle N_0 \rangle \to \infty$.

Problem 3: Density of States at Zero Energy (Q3)

Answer: (b).

The density of states $g(\epsilon) = \frac{\pi}{4\epsilon_1^{3/2}}\sqrt{\epsilon}$ vanishes at $\epsilon = 0$, so the integral $N = \int_0^\infty g(\epsilon)\langle n_\epsilon\rangle\, d\epsilon$ assigns zero weight to the ground state. We must split $N = \langle N_\text{ground}\rangle + \langle N_\text{excited}\rangle$ and handle the ground state term separately.

Problem 4: Scaling of $T_c$ with Density (Q4)

Answer: (b) $T_c \propto (N/V)^{2/3}$.

Eq. (33) gives $T_c = 3.31\frac{\hbar^2}{k_B m}\left(\frac{N}{V}\right)^{2/3}$.

Problem 5: Two-State Low-Temperature Estimate (Q5)

From Eq. (12): $\frac{\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle}{N} = 1 - \frac{k_BT}{N\epsilon}$.

Setting $\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle / N = 0.99$:

$$0.99 = 1 - \frac{k_BT}{N\epsilon} \implies \frac{k_BT}{N\epsilon} = 0.01$$ $$k_BT = 0.01 \times N\epsilon = 0.01 \times 10^6 \epsilon = 10^4\,\epsilon$$

So $T = 10^4\,\epsilon/k_B$. This is an astronomically high temperature compared to the energy gap $\epsilon$, demonstrating the dramatic effect of Bose-Einstein condensation: 99% of the particles are in the ground state even when $k_BT$ is ten thousand times larger than the gap.

Problem 6: $T_c$ for Rubidium BEC (Q6)

Using Eq. (33): $T_c = 3.31\frac{\hbar^2}{k_B m}\left(\frac{N}{V}\right)^{2/3}$.

Given: $N = 10^{12}$, $V = 1\,\text{cm}^3 = 10^{-6}\,\text{m}^3$, $m = 1.44 \times 10^{-25}\,\text{kg}$.

$$\frac{N}{V} = \frac{10^{12}}{10^{-6}} = 10^{18}\,\text{m}^{-3}$$ $$\left(\frac{N}{V}\right)^{2/3} = (10^{18})^{2/3} = 10^{12}\,\text{m}^{-2}$$ $$T_c = 3.31 \times \frac{(1.055 \times 10^{-34})^2}{1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 1.44 \times 10^{-25}} \times 10^{12}$$ $$= 3.31 \times \frac{1.113 \times 10^{-68}}{1.989 \times 10^{-48}} \times 10^{12}$$ $$= 3.31 \times 5.60 \times 10^{-21} \times 10^{12}$$ $$= 3.31 \times 5.60 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{K}$$ $$\boxed{T_c \approx 18.5\,\text{nK}}$$

This is in the nanokelvin range, consistent with the experimental observations described in the chapter where $T_c \approx 170\,\text{nK}$ was achieved with somewhat different densities.

Problem 7: Half-Condensate Temperature (Q7)

From Eq. (35): $\frac{N_0}{N} = 1 - \left(\frac{T}{T_c}\right)^{3/2}$.

Setting $N_0/N = 1/2$:

$$\frac{1}{2} = 1 - \left(\frac{T}{T_c}\right)^{3/2} \implies \left(\frac{T}{T_c}\right)^{3/2} = \frac{1}{2}$$ $$\frac{T}{T_c} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2/3} = 2^{-2/3} \approx 0.630$$ $$\boxed{T \approx 0.630\,T_c}$$

At this temperature, $1/2$ of the particles are in excited states.

Problem 8: Chemical Potential vs Ground State Occupation (Q8)

From Eq. (15): $e^{-\beta\mu} = \frac{1}{\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle} + 1$, so $\beta\mu = -\ln\left(\frac{1}{\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle} + 1\right)$.

For $\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle = 1000$:

$$\beta\mu = -\ln\left(\frac{1}{1000} + 1\right) = -\ln(1.001) \approx -0.001$$ $$\boxed{\mu \approx -0.001\,k_BT}$$

For $\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle = 10$:

$$\beta\mu = -\ln\left(\frac{1}{10} + 1\right) = -\ln(1.1) \approx -0.0953$$ $$\boxed{\mu \approx -0.095\,k_BT}$$

When the ground state is highly occupied (BEC regime), $\mu$ is extremely close to zero. As $\langle N_\text{ground}\rangle$ decreases, $\mu$ becomes more negative. This confirms that the $\mu \approx 0$ approximation is excellent in the condensed phase.

Problem 9: Two-State BEC Detailed Analysis
(a)

The partition function is $Z_N^\text{BE} = \sum_{m=0}^{N} e^{-m\beta\epsilon} = \frac{1 - e^{-(N+1)\beta\epsilon}}{1 - e^{-\beta\epsilon}}$.

The average energy is:

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z_N^\text{BE}}{\partial \beta}$$

We can also compute it directly. The energy when $m$ particles are in the excited state is $m\epsilon$. So:

$$\langle E \rangle = \frac{1}{Z_N^\text{BE}} \sum_{m=0}^{N} m\epsilon\, e^{-m\beta\epsilon}$$

Let $x = e^{-\beta\epsilon}$. Then $Z = \sum_{m=0}^N x^m = \frac{1 - x^{N+1}}{1-x}$ and:

$$\langle E \rangle = \frac{\epsilon}{Z} \sum_{m=0}^N m\, x^m = \frac{\epsilon}{Z}\left(-\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon}\frac{\partial Z}{\partial \epsilon}\cdot(-1)\right) = -\frac{1}{Z}\frac{\partial Z}{\partial \beta}$$

Computing $-\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta}$:

$$\langle E \rangle = \epsilon\left[\frac{1}{e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1} - \frac{(N+1)}{e^{(N+1)\beta\epsilon} - 1}\right]$$

This is the difference of two Bose-Einstein-like terms.

(b)

The heat capacity is $C = \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial T} = -k_B\beta^2 \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial \beta}$.

$$C = k_B(\beta\epsilon)^2\left[\frac{e^{\beta\epsilon}}{(e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1)^2} - \frac{(N+1)^2 e^{(N+1)\beta\epsilon}}{(e^{(N+1)\beta\epsilon} - 1)^2}\right]$$

For $N = 100$, the heat capacity shows a peak near $k_BT \sim N\epsilon/6$, corresponding to the BEC transition. Below this temperature, $C$ is small because most particles are frozen in the ground state. Above the transition, $C$ decreases as both states become equally populated.

(c)

For Maxwell-Boltzmann: from Eq. (7), $\langle N_\text{excited}^\text{MB}\rangle = N - \langle N_\text{ground}^\text{MB}\rangle = \frac{Ne^{-\beta\epsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta\epsilon}}$.

The average energy is:

$$\langle E^\text{MB} \rangle = \langle N_\text{excited}^\text{MB}\rangle \cdot \epsilon = \frac{N\epsilon}{e^{\beta\epsilon} + 1}$$

The heat capacity is:

$$C^\text{MB} = Nk_B \frac{(\beta\epsilon)^2 e^{\beta\epsilon}}{(e^{\beta\epsilon} + 1)^2}$$

This is $N$ times the Schottky anomaly for a two-level system. It has a single broad peak at $k_BT \approx 0.42\epsilon$ and no phase transition. Key differences: (1) The MB peak occurs at $k_BT \sim \epsilon$, while the BE peak occurs at $k_BT \sim N\epsilon$, vastly higher. (2) The BE heat capacity shows a sharper feature associated with the phase transition. (3) Both approach zero at low $T$ and high $T$.

(d)

In the limit $k_BT \gg N\epsilon$ (i.e., $\beta\epsilon \ll 1/N$), expand to leading order. For BE:

$$\langle E^\text{BE} \rangle \approx \epsilon\left[\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon} - \frac{(N+1)}{(N+1)\beta\epsilon}\right] + \cdots = \epsilon\left[\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon} - \frac{1}{\beta\epsilon}\right] + \cdots$$

We need to go to higher order. Expanding more carefully using $\frac{1}{e^x - 1} \approx \frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{x}{12} + \cdots$:

$$\langle E^\text{BE} \rangle \approx \epsilon\left[\left(\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon} - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\beta\epsilon}{12}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{(N+1)\beta\epsilon} - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{(N+1)\beta\epsilon}{12}\right)\right]$$ $$= \epsilon\left[\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon}\left(1 - \frac{1}{N+1}\right) - \frac{\beta\epsilon}{12}(N+1 - 1)\right]$$ $$= \frac{N}{N+1}\frac{1}{\beta} - \frac{N(\beta\epsilon)\epsilon}{12}$$

For MB: $\langle E^\text{MB}\rangle = \frac{N\epsilon}{e^{\beta\epsilon} + 1} \approx \frac{N\epsilon}{2 + \beta\epsilon} \approx \frac{N\epsilon}{2}\left(1 - \frac{\beta\epsilon}{2}\right)$.

To leading order for large $N$, both give $\langle E \rangle \approx \frac{N\epsilon}{2}$ (equipartition among two states). The leading corrections differ: the BE correction involves a term $\propto N(\beta\epsilon)^2$ while the MB correction goes as $N(\beta\epsilon)$.

Problem 10: BEC in a Harmonic Trap
(a)

The number of states with total quantum number $n = n_x + n_y + n_z$ is the number of ways to write $n$ as an ordered sum of three non-negative integers, which is $\binom{n+2}{2} = \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2}$.

For large $n$, this is approximately $\frac{n^2}{2}$. The energy is $\epsilon = \hbar\omega\, n$, so $n = \epsilon/(\hbar\omega)$ and $dn = d\epsilon/(\hbar\omega)$. Thus:

$$g(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon = \frac{n^2}{2}\,dn = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\epsilon}{\hbar\omega}\right)^2 \frac{d\epsilon}{\hbar\omega}$$ $$\boxed{g(\epsilon) = \frac{\epsilon^2}{2(\hbar\omega)^3}}$$
(b)

Setting $\mu = 0$ for the excited states:

$$\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = \int_0^\infty g(\epsilon)\frac{1}{e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1}\,d\epsilon = \frac{1}{2(\hbar\omega)^3}\int_0^\infty \frac{\epsilon^2}{e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1}\,d\epsilon$$

Substituting $u = \beta\epsilon$:

$$= \frac{1}{2(\hbar\omega)^3}\left(\frac{1}{\beta}\right)^3\int_0^\infty \frac{u^2}{e^u - 1}\,du = \frac{(k_BT)^3}{2(\hbar\omega)^3}\cdot 2\zeta(3)$$

The integral $\int_0^\infty \frac{u^2}{e^u - 1}\,du = 2\zeta(3)$ where $\zeta(3) \approx 1.202$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = \zeta(3)\left(\frac{k_BT}{\hbar\omega}\right)^3}$$
(c)

At $T = T_c$, $\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = N$:

$$N = \zeta(3)\left(\frac{k_BT_c}{\hbar\omega}\right)^3$$ $$\boxed{T_c = \frac{\hbar\omega}{k_B}\left(\frac{N}{\zeta(3)}\right)^{1/3} \approx \frac{0.94\,\hbar\omega}{k_B}N^{1/3}}$$

In the harmonic trap, $T_c \propto N^{1/3}$, compared to $T_c \propto N^{2/3}$ for the box (via $T_c \propto (N/V)^{2/3}$ with $V = L^3$ fixed). The different scaling arises because the density of states has a different energy dependence: $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^2$ for the trap vs $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^{1/2}$ for the box.

(d)

Below $T_c$:

$$\frac{N_0}{N} = 1 - \frac{\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle}{N} = 1 - \frac{\zeta(3)(k_BT/\hbar\omega)^3}{\zeta(3)(k_BT_c/\hbar\omega)^3} = 1 - \left(\frac{T}{T_c}\right)^3$$ $$\boxed{\frac{N_0}{N} = 1 - \left(\frac{T}{T_c}\right)^3}$$

The exponent is 3 instead of $3/2$ because the density of states for the harmonic trap is $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^2$ (steeper than $\epsilon^{1/2}$ for the box). The general rule is: if $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^{\alpha}$, then $\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle \propto T^{\alpha+1}$ and the condensate fraction goes as $1 - (T/T_c)^{\alpha+1}$. For the box, $\alpha = 1/2$ gives exponent $3/2$; for the trap, $\alpha = 2$ gives exponent $3$.

Problem 11: Superfluid Helium-4
(a)

Number density:

$$n = \frac{\rho}{m} = \frac{145}{6.65 \times 10^{-27}} = 2.18 \times 10^{28}\,\text{m}^{-3}$$

Using Eq. (33):

$$T_c = 3.31\frac{\hbar^2}{k_B m}\left(\frac{N}{V}\right)^{2/3} = 3.31 \times \frac{(1.055 \times 10^{-34})^2}{1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 6.65 \times 10^{-27}} \times (2.18 \times 10^{28})^{2/3}$$ $$= 3.31 \times \frac{1.113 \times 10^{-68}}{9.18 \times 10^{-50}} \times (2.18 \times 10^{28})^{2/3}$$ $$= 3.31 \times 1.212 \times 10^{-19} \times 7.81 \times 10^{18}$$ $$\boxed{T_c \approx 3.13\,\text{K}}$$

This is somewhat higher than $T_\lambda = 2.17\,\text{K}$, but remarkably close given that liquid helium is far from an ideal gas. The factor of ~1.4 discrepancy is due to interatomic interactions in the liquid, which are neglected in the ideal gas treatment.

(b)

Using Eq. (35) with $T_c = 2.17\,\text{K}$ and $T = 1\,\text{K}$:

$$\frac{N_0}{N} = 1 - \left(\frac{1}{2.17}\right)^{3/2} = 1 - (0.461)^{3/2} = 1 - 0.313 = 0.687$$

The ideal gas predicts $\approx 69\%$ condensate fraction at 1 K, and 100% at $T = 0$.

Experimentally, the condensate fraction is only about 7-8% even at $T \to 0$. The enormous discrepancy arises because liquid helium has strong interatomic interactions (van der Waals forces). These interactions scatter particles out of the zero-momentum condensate into finite-momentum states (quantum depletion). The superfluid fraction (which is close to 100% at $T = 0$) is not the same as the condensate fraction; superfluidity involves coherent flow of all particles, not just those in the $k = 0$ state.

(c)
$$\lambda_\text{dB} = \sqrt{\frac{2\pi\hbar^2}{mk_BT_\lambda}} = \sqrt{\frac{2\pi(1.055 \times 10^{-34})^2}{6.65 \times 10^{-27} \times 1.381 \times 10^{-23} \times 2.17}}$$ $$= \sqrt{\frac{6.99 \times 10^{-68}}{1.99 \times 10^{-49}}} = \sqrt{3.51 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 5.93 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{m}$$ $$\boxed{\lambda_\text{dB} \approx 0.59\,\text{nm}}$$

Mean interparticle spacing:

$$d = n^{-1/3} = (2.18 \times 10^{28})^{-1/3} = 3.57 \times 10^{-10}\,\text{m} \approx 0.36\,\text{nm}$$

The ratio $\lambda_\text{dB}/d \approx 1.7$, so $\lambda_\text{dB} \sim d$. The thermal de Broglie wavelength is comparable to the interparticle spacing, confirming that quantum effects (wave function overlap) are important at the lambda point.

(d)

$^3\text{He}$ has an odd total number of fermions (2 protons + 1 neutron + 2 electrons = 5 fermions), making the atom itself a fermion with half-integer spin. Fermions obey the Pauli exclusion principle and cannot macroscopically occupy a single quantum state, so single $^3\text{He}$ atoms cannot undergo BEC.

However, at sufficiently low temperatures ($T \approx 2.5\,\text{mK}$), pairs of $^3\text{He}$ atoms can form bound states analogous to Cooper pairs in superconductors. A pair of fermions is a boson (the total spin is integer), so these pairs can undergo BEC, leading to superfluidity. The pairing is mediated by spin fluctuations rather than phonons (as in electronic superconductors). The much lower transition temperature compared to $^4\text{He}$ reflects the weakness of the pairing interaction and the fact that the pairs must first form before they can condense.

Problem 12: BEC in Lower Dimensions
(a)

In $d$ dimensions, $\vec{n} = (n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_d)$ with each $n_i \geq 0$. Converting the sum to an integral:

$$\sum_{\vec{n}} \to \frac{1}{2^d}\int d^d n = \frac{1}{2^d} S_d \int_0^\infty n^{d-1}\,dn$$

where $S_d = \frac{2\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2)}$ is the full $d$-dimensional solid angle (we take the $1/2^d$ factor for the positive-$n$ octant). Using $\epsilon = \epsilon_1 n^2$, so $n = \sqrt{\epsilon/\epsilon_1}$ and $dn = \frac{d\epsilon}{2\sqrt{\epsilon_1\epsilon}}$:

$$g(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon = \frac{S_d}{2^d}\left(\frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_1}\right)^{(d-1)/2}\frac{d\epsilon}{2\sqrt{\epsilon_1\epsilon}}$$ $$= \frac{S_d}{2^{d+1}\epsilon_1^{d/2}}\epsilon^{(d-2)/2}\,d\epsilon = \frac{S_d}{2^{d+1}\epsilon_1^{d/2}}\epsilon^{d/2 - 1}\,d\epsilon$$ $$\boxed{g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^{d/2 - 1}}$$

For $d = 3$: $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^{1/2}$ (as in the chapter). For $d = 2$: $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^0 = \text{const}$. For $d = 1$: $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon^{-1/2}$.

(b)

With $\mu = 0$:

$$\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle \propto \int_0^\infty \frac{\epsilon^{d/2-1}}{e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1}\,d\epsilon$$

Near $\epsilon = 0$, $e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1 \approx \beta\epsilon$, so the integrand behaves as:

$$\frac{\epsilon^{d/2-1}}{\beta\epsilon} = \frac{\epsilon^{d/2-2}}{\beta}$$

This integrand is integrable near $\epsilon = 0$ if $d/2 - 2 > -1$, i.e., $d > 2$. For $d \leq 2$, the integral diverges at the lower limit.

More explicitly: for $d = 2$, the integrand near zero goes as $\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon}$, and $\int_0 \frac{d\epsilon}{\epsilon} = \ln\epsilon \to -\infty$. For $d = 1$, it goes as $\frac{1}{\beta\epsilon^{3/2}}$, which diverges even faster.

For $d = 3$, the integrand goes as $\epsilon^{-1/2}$, which is integrable, and the full integral gives $\Gamma(3/2)\zeta(3/2)/\beta^{3/2}$, a finite result.

(c)

The divergence of $\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle$ for $d \leq 2$ means that the excited states can accommodate an unlimited number of particles even with $\mu = 0$. In the chapter's derivation, the critical temperature was found by setting $\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = N$ with $\mu = 0$. If $\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle$ diverges for $\mu = 0$, then excited states can always absorb all $N$ particles without the chemical potential needing to approach zero. Since $\mu$ never reaches zero, the ground state occupation $\langle N_0\rangle = 1/(e^{-\beta\mu} - 1)$ stays finite and there is no condensation.

Physically, in lower dimensions, the density of low-energy states is large enough that thermal fluctuations can spread particles across infinitely many low-lying excited states, preventing the macroscopic accumulation in the ground state that defines BEC.

(d)

For a 2D harmonic trap, the single-particle energies are $\epsilon = \hbar\omega(n_x + n_y)$ with $n_x, n_y = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$. The number of states at energy level $n = n_x + n_y$ is $n + 1 \approx n$ for large $n$. With $\epsilon = \hbar\omega\, n$:

$$g(\epsilon) = \frac{n}{\hbar\omega} = \frac{\epsilon}{(\hbar\omega)^2}$$

So $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon$, which is steeper than the $g(\epsilon) = \text{const}$ for the 2D box. Now:

$$\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = \int_0^\infty \frac{\epsilon}{(\hbar\omega)^2}\frac{1}{e^{\beta\epsilon} - 1}\,d\epsilon = \frac{1}{(\hbar\omega)^2}\left(\frac{1}{\beta}\right)^2\int_0^\infty \frac{u}{e^u - 1}\,du$$

The integral $\int_0^\infty \frac{u}{e^u - 1}\,du = \Gamma(2)\zeta(2) = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$, which is finite. Therefore:

$$\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = \frac{\pi^2}{6}\left(\frac{k_BT}{\hbar\omega}\right)^2$$

Setting $\langle N_\text{excited}\rangle = N$:

$$\boxed{T_c = \frac{\hbar\omega}{k_B}\sqrt{\frac{6N}{\pi^2}} = \frac{\hbar\omega}{k_B}\sqrt{\frac{N}{\zeta(2)}}}$$

BEC occurs because the harmonic potential compresses the density of states at low energies compared to the box. The box in 2D has $g(\epsilon) = \text{const}$, which gives a divergent integral, but the trap has $g(\epsilon) \propto \epsilon$, which provides sufficient suppression of low-energy states for the integral to converge. This shows that the existence of BEC depends not only on dimensionality but also on the shape of the confining potential.
