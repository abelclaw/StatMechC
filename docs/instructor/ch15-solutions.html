Chapter 15: Stars — Solutions

Q1: Vogt-Russell Parameter

Answer: (c) Mass $M$. The Vogt-Russell theorem states that all stars with the same chemical composition and mass are identical. Therefore mass is the single parameter that indexes the family.

Q2: Hydrogen Fusion and Tunneling

Answer: (b) Quantum tunneling. The classical minimum temperature for protons to overcome the Coulomb barrier is $T_{\min}^{\text{classical}} = 4.2 \times 10^9\;\text{K}$ (Eq. 11), far above the sun's core temperature of $\sim 15 \times 10^6\;\text{K}$. Quantum tunneling allows protons to penetrate the barrier with a de Broglie wavelength $\lambda = h/(m_p v)$, reducing the effective minimum temperature to $\sim 20 \times 10^6\;\text{K}$.

Q3: White Dwarf Support

Answer: (c) Electron degeneracy pressure. White dwarfs have no nuclear fusion. They are supported entirely by electron degeneracy pressure from the Pauli exclusion principle. The electrons form a degenerate Fermi gas with $T \ll T_F$.

Q4: Eddington Model Assumption

Answer: (b) The ratio of radiation pressure to gas pressure is constant throughout the star. Writing $P_{\text{rad}} = \beta P_{\text{tot}}$ with $\beta$ independent of $r$, one can eliminate $T$ between $P_{\text{gas}} = \frac{\rho}{\mu m_p}k_B T$ and $P_{\text{rad}} = \frac{4\sigma}{3c}T^4$ to obtain $P \propto \rho^{4/3}$, an $n = 3$ polytrope (Eq. 26).

Q5: Stellar Radius from Stefan-Boltzmann

From the Stefan-Boltzmann law, $L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T_s^4$. Taking ratios with the sun:

$$\frac{L}{L_\odot} = \left(\frac{R}{R_\odot}\right)^2\left(\frac{T_s}{T_{s,\odot}}\right)^4$$

With $L/L_\odot = 64$ and $T_s/T_{s,\odot} = 2$:

$$64 = \left(\frac{R}{R_\odot}\right)^2 \times 2^4 = 16\left(\frac{R}{R_\odot}\right)^2$$ $$\frac{R}{R_\odot} = \sqrt{\frac{64}{16}} = \sqrt{4} = \boxed{2}$$
Q6: Scaling of the Chandrasekhar Mass

Answer: (c) Increasing the speed of light $c$. The Chandrasekhar mass scales as $M_{\text{Ch}} \propto c^{3/2}\hbar^{3/2}G^{-3/2}m_p^{-2}$. Increasing $c$ increases $M_{\text{Ch}}$ since it appears with a positive exponent. Increasing $G$ ($M_{\text{Ch}} \propto G^{-3/2}$) or $m_p$ ($M_{\text{Ch}} \propto m_p^{-2}$) would decrease it. Decreasing $\hbar$ ($M_{\text{Ch}} \propto \hbar^{3/2}$) would also decrease it.

Q7: Core Temperature Estimate

From Eq. (7) with $M_{\text{shell}} \approx M = 4M_\odot$:

$$k_B T_{\text{core}} = \frac{1}{3}\frac{G M m_p}{r_{\text{core}}} = \frac{1}{3}\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(4 \times 2 \times 10^{30})(1.67 \times 10^{-27})}{7 \times 10^8}$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}\frac{6.67 \times 8 \times 1.67 \times 10^{-11+30-27}}{7 \times 10^8} = \frac{1}{3}\frac{89.1 \times 10^{-8}}{7 \times 10^8}$$ $$= \frac{89.1}{21} \times 10^{-16} = 4.24 \times 10^{-16}\;\text{J}$$ $$T_{\text{core}} = \frac{4.24 \times 10^{-16}}{1.38 \times 10^{-23}} = 30.7 \times 10^6\;\text{K} \approx \boxed{31 \times 10^6\;\text{K}}$$

This is simply 4 times the solar estimate of $7.7 \times 10^6\;\text{K}$ since $T_{\text{core}} \propto M$.

Q8: Neutron Star Density

The average density is:

$$\bar{\rho} = \frac{M}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3} = \frac{1.4 \times 2 \times 10^{30}\;\text{kg}}{\frac{4}{3}\pi (10^4\;\text{m})^3}$$ $$= \frac{2.8 \times 10^{30}}{4.189 \times 10^{12}} = 6.68 \times 10^{17}\;\text{kg/m}^3$$

So $a = \boxed{6.7}$. This exceeds the density of nuclear matter ($\sim 10^{17}\;\text{kg/m}^3$).

Problem 1: The Jeans Instability
(a)

The mass of a uniform sphere of density $\rho$ and radius $R$ is:

$$M = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \rho$$

Substituting into the gravitational energy:

$$|E_{\text{grav}}| = \frac{3}{5}\frac{GM^2}{R} = \frac{3}{5}\frac{G\left(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \rho\right)^2}{R} = \frac{16\pi^2}{15}G\rho^2 R^5$$
(b)

The thermal energy is:

$$E_{\text{therm}} = \frac{3}{2}\frac{M}{m_p}k_B T = \frac{3}{2}\frac{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \rho}{m_p}k_B T = 2\pi R^3 \frac{\rho k_B T}{m_p}$$

Setting $|E_{\text{grav}}| > E_{\text{therm}}$:

$$\frac{16\pi^2}{15}G\rho^2 R^5 > 2\pi R^3 \frac{\rho k_B T}{m_p}$$ $$R^2 > \frac{2\pi \times 15}{16\pi^2}\frac{k_B T}{G\rho m_p} = \frac{15 k_B T}{8\pi G \rho m_p}$$

Using $\rho = n m_p$:

$$R^2 > \frac{15 k_B T}{8\pi G m_p^2 n}$$

Wait -- let me recheck. We need:

$$\frac{16\pi^2}{15}G\rho^2 R^5 > 2\pi R^3 \frac{\rho k_B T}{m_p}$$ $$R^2 > \frac{2\pi \times 15}{16\pi^2} \frac{k_B T}{G \rho m_p} = \frac{15}{8\pi}\frac{k_B T}{G \rho m_p}$$

With $\rho = nm_p$:

$$R^2 > \frac{15 k_B T}{8\pi G m_p^2 n}$$

Hmm, this gives $R_J = \sqrt{\frac{15 k_B T}{8\pi G m_p^2 n}}$. Let us recheck the factor. Actually, the thermal energy per particle for a monatomic ideal gas is $\frac{3}{2}k_BT$, but the number of particles is $N = M/m_p = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 n$. So:

$$E_{\text{therm}} = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT = \frac{3}{2}\cdot\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 n \cdot k_BT = 2\pi R^3 n k_B T$$

And $|E_{\text{grav}}| = \frac{3}{5}\frac{G(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 nm_p)^2}{R} = \frac{16\pi^2}{15}Gn^2m_p^2 R^5$.

Setting them equal:

$$\frac{16\pi^2}{15}Gn^2m_p^2 R_J^5 = 2\pi R_J^3 n k_B T$$ $$R_J^2 = \frac{30\pi n k_B T}{16\pi^2 G n^2 m_p^2} = \frac{15 k_B T}{8\pi G m_p^2 n}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{R_J = \sqrt{\frac{15 k_B T}{8\pi G m_p^2 n}}}$$

(Note: the factor differs from some conventions by factors of order unity depending on whether one uses the sound-crossing argument or the energy argument. The problem statement uses the convention $R_J = \sqrt{\frac{15 k_B T}{4\pi G m_p^2 n}}$, which corresponds to using $E_{\text{therm}} = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$ with $N = M/(2m_p)$ for molecular hydrogen $\text{H}_2$, where each molecule has mass $2m_p$. With molecular hydrogen, $N = M/(2m_p) = \frac{2}{3}\pi R^3 n$ and $E_{\text{therm}} = \pi R^3 n k_B T$, giving the stated result.)

(c)

Using the Jeans length from part (b) with the molecular hydrogen convention:

$$R_J = \sqrt{\frac{15 k_B T}{4\pi G m_p^2 n}}$$

Plugging in $T = 10\;\text{K}$, $n = 10^{10}\;\text{m}^{-3}$:

$$R_J = \sqrt{\frac{15 \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 10}{4\pi \times 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times (1.67 \times 10^{-27})^2 \times 10^{10}}}$$ $$= \sqrt{\frac{2.07 \times 10^{-21}}{2.34 \times 10^{-54}}} = \sqrt{8.85 \times 10^{32}} = 2.97 \times 10^{16}\;\text{m}$$

This is about 1 parsec ($3.09 \times 10^{16}\;\text{m}$). The Jeans mass is:

$$M_J = \frac{4}{3}\pi R_J^3 \rho = \frac{4}{3}\pi (2.97 \times 10^{16})^3 \times (10^{10} \times 1.67 \times 10^{-27})$$ $$= \frac{4}{3}\pi \times 2.62 \times 10^{49} \times 1.67 \times 10^{-17} = 1.83 \times 10^{33}\;\text{kg}$$ $$M_J = \frac{1.83 \times 10^{33}}{2 \times 10^{30}} \approx \boxed{900\,M_\odot}$$

This is much larger than a single star, consistent with the fact that collapsing clouds fragment into many stars.

(d)

Higher temperature means greater thermal kinetic energy opposing gravitational collapse. From the Jeans length, $R_J \propto T^{1/2}$, so $M_J \propto R_J^3 \rho \propto T^{3/2}$. Hotter gas requires a larger, more massive region to initiate collapse. In Interactive 2, increasing the temperature slider increases the thermal pressure resisting collapse, making it harder for dense regions to reach the Jeans threshold and form protostars. At sufficiently high temperature, even the protostars that have begun to form can evaporate.

Problem 2: Solving the Lane-Emden Equation
(a)

For $n = 0$, the RHS is $-\theta^0 = -1$. The Lane-Emden equation becomes:

$$\frac{1}{\xi^2}\frac{d}{d\xi}\left(\xi^2\frac{d\theta}{d\xi}\right) = -1$$

Integrating once: $\xi^2\theta' = -\xi^3/3 + C_1$. The boundary condition $\theta'(0) = 0$ requires $C_1 = 0$, so $\theta' = -\xi/3$. Integrating again: $\theta = -\xi^2/6 + C_2$. The condition $\theta(0) = 1$ gives $C_2 = 1$.

$$\boxed{\theta(\xi) = 1 - \frac{\xi^2}{6}}$$

Setting $\theta = 0$: $\xi_{\max}^2 = 6$, so $\xi_{\max} = \sqrt{6} \approx 2.449$.

(b)

For $n = 1$, we need to verify $\theta = \frac{\sin\xi}{\xi}$ satisfies:

$$\frac{1}{\xi^2}\frac{d}{d\xi}\left(\xi^2\frac{d\theta}{d\xi}\right) = -\theta$$

Compute $\theta' = \frac{\xi\cos\xi - \sin\xi}{\xi^2}$. Then:

$$\xi^2\theta' = \xi\cos\xi - \sin\xi$$ $$\frac{d}{d\xi}(\xi^2\theta') = \cos\xi - \xi\sin\xi - \cos\xi = -\xi\sin\xi$$ $$\frac{1}{\xi^2}\frac{d}{d\xi}(\xi^2\theta') = -\frac{\sin\xi}{\xi} = -\theta \quad \checkmark$$

Boundary conditions: As $\xi \to 0$, $\theta = \frac{\sin\xi}{\xi} \to 1$ by L'Hôpital's rule. Also $\theta'(0) = \lim_{\xi \to 0}\frac{\xi\cos\xi - \sin\xi}{\xi^2} = \lim_{\xi \to 0}\frac{-\xi\sin\xi}{\xi^2} = 0$ (by L'Hôpital or Taylor expansion). Both conditions are satisfied.

The first zero of $\sin\xi/\xi$ occurs at $\xi_{\max} = \pi$.

(c)

Let $\theta = 1 + a_2\xi^2 + a_4\xi^4 + \cdots$. We need to expand both sides of the Lane-Emden equation.

Left side: $\frac{1}{\xi^2}\frac{d}{d\xi}\left(\xi^2(2a_2\xi + 4a_4\xi^3 + \cdots)\right) = \frac{1}{\xi^2}\frac{d}{d\xi}(2a_2\xi^3 + 4a_4\xi^5 + \cdots)$

$$= \frac{1}{\xi^2}(6a_2\xi^2 + 20a_4\xi^4 + \cdots) = 6a_2 + 20a_4\xi^2 + \cdots$$

Right side: $-\theta^n = -(1 + a_2\xi^2 + \cdots)^n = -1 - na_2\xi^2 - \left(na_4 + \frac{n(n-1)}{2}a_2^2\right)\xi^4 - \cdots$

Matching constant terms: $6a_2 = -1$, so $\boxed{a_2 = -1/6}$ for all $n$.

Matching $\xi^2$ terms: $20a_4 = -na_2 = n/6$, so:

$$\boxed{a_4 = \frac{n}{120}}$$
(d)

The mean density is $\bar{\rho} = M/(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3) = C_n \rho_c$ from Eq. (31). So $\rho_c/\bar{\rho} = 1/C_n$.

For $n = 0$: The density is constant ($\rho = \rho_c$ everywhere since $\theta^0 = 1$), so $\rho_c/\bar{\rho} = 1$. We can verify: $C_0 = \frac{4\pi}{\xi_{\max}^3}\int_0^{\xi_{\max}}\xi^2 d\xi = \frac{4\pi}{3\xi_{\max}^3}\xi_{\max}^3 = \frac{4\pi}{3}$... Actually, from the definition $M = C_n R^3 \rho_c$, and for constant density $M = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \rho_c$, we have $C_0 = 4\pi/3$. But $\bar{\rho} = M/(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3) = C_0 \rho_c \cdot R^3 / (\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3)$... Let me reconsider. From Eq. (31), $M = C_n R^3 \rho_c$. The mean density is $\bar{\rho} = 3M/(4\pi R^3) = 3C_n\rho_c/(4\pi)$. But wait -- looking at the table, $C_0$ is not listed. For constant density, $\rho_c/\bar\rho = 1$ trivially.

For $n = 1$: $\bar{\rho} = \frac{3M}{4\pi R^3}$. We need $M = 4\pi\int_0^R \rho_c \frac{\sin(\xi)}{\xi} r^2 dr$. With $r = r_0\xi$ and $R = r_0\pi$:

$$M = 4\pi r_0^3 \rho_c \int_0^\pi \frac{\sin\xi}{\xi}\xi^2 d\xi = 4\pi r_0^3 \rho_c \int_0^\pi \xi\sin\xi\,d\xi$$ $$= 4\pi r_0^3 \rho_c [-\xi\cos\xi + \sin\xi]_0^\pi = 4\pi r_0^3 \rho_c \cdot \pi = 4\pi^2 r_0^3 \rho_c$$

The mean density: $\bar{\rho} = \frac{3M}{4\pi R^3} = \frac{3 \times 4\pi^2 r_0^3 \rho_c}{4\pi \times \pi^3 r_0^3} = \frac{3\rho_c}{\pi^2}$.

$$\frac{\rho_c}{\bar{\rho}} = \frac{\pi^2}{3} \approx 3.29$$

So the $n = 1$ polytrope is more centrally concentrated than $n = 0$.

For $n = 3$: From the table, $C_3 = 0.077$. The mean density is $\bar{\rho} = 3C_3\rho_c/(4\pi) \cdot (4\pi/3) = C_3 \rho_c \cdot 3/(4\pi) \cdot 4\pi/3$... Let me be more careful. $M = C_3 R^3 \rho_c$ and $\bar{\rho} = M/(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3)$, so $\bar{\rho} = \frac{3C_3}{4\pi}\rho_c$. Therefore $\rho_c/\bar{\rho} = 4\pi/(3C_3) = 4\pi/(3 \times 0.077) = 54.4$. The $n = 3$ star is much more centrally concentrated, with a core density 54 times its mean density.

Problem 3: White Dwarf Properties
(a)

For a non-relativistic white dwarf ($n = 3/2$ polytrope), $R \propto M^{-1/3}$. Given $R(0.5\,M_\odot) \approx R_E$:

$$\frac{R(0.6)}{R(0.5)} = \left(\frac{0.6}{0.5}\right)^{-1/3} = (1.2)^{-1/3} = 0.938$$ $$R(0.6\,M_\odot) \approx 0.94\,R_E$$
(b)

Using $R = 0.94\,R_E = 0.94 \times 6370\;\text{km} = 5990\;\text{km} = 5.99 \times 10^6\;\text{m}$:

$$\bar{\rho} = \frac{M}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3} = \frac{0.6 \times 2 \times 10^{30}}{\frac{4}{3}\pi (5.99 \times 10^6)^3} = \frac{1.2 \times 10^{30}}{9.00 \times 10^{20}} = 1.33 \times 10^9\;\text{kg/m}^3$$

This is $\bar{\rho}/\rho_\odot = 1.33 \times 10^9 / 1400 \approx 10^6$, about a million times denser than the sun.

(c)

The electron number density:

$$n_e = \frac{\bar{\rho}}{2m_p} = \frac{1.33 \times 10^9}{2 \times 1.67 \times 10^{-27}} = 3.98 \times 10^{35}\;\text{m}^{-3}$$

The non-relativistic Fermi energy:

$$\epsilon_F = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}(3\pi^2 n_e)^{2/3} = \frac{(1.055 \times 10^{-34})^2}{2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31}}(3\pi^2 \times 3.98 \times 10^{35})^{2/3}$$ $$= 6.11 \times 10^{-39} \times (1.18 \times 10^{37})^{2/3} = 6.11 \times 10^{-39} \times 5.15 \times 10^{24}$$ $$= 3.15 \times 10^{-14}\;\text{J} = 197\;\text{keV}$$

Since $\epsilon_F \approx 200\;\text{keV}$ is comparable to $m_e c^2 = 511\;\text{keV}$ (same order of magnitude), relativistic corrections are becoming important for this white dwarf. A fully non-relativistic treatment is only marginally valid.

(d)

From Eq. (81):

$$x_F = 0.97\left(\frac{R_E}{R}\right)\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)^{1/3} = 0.97 \times \frac{R_E}{0.54\,R_E} \times (1.02)^{1/3}$$ $$= 0.97 \times 1.852 \times 1.007 = 1.81$$

Since $x_F \approx 1.8$, Sirius B is mildly relativistic ($x_F \sim 1$). This confirms that the full relativistic treatment is needed to accurately model Sirius B, consistent with its position near the relativistic curve in Figure 3.

Problem 4: Neutron Stars and Pulsars
(a)

From Eqs. (85) and (87), the characteristic radii are:

$$R_{\text{WD}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{\hbar^3}{cG^3 m_e^2 m_p^2}}\,,\qquad R_{\text{NS}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{4\hbar^3}{cG^3 m_p^4}}$$

The ratio (dropping factors of order unity like $\sqrt{4} = 2$):

$$\frac{R_{\text{WD}}}{R_{\text{NS}}} \sim \frac{m_p}{m_e} = \frac{1836\,m_e}{m_e} = 1836$$

More precisely, $R_{\text{WD}} / R_{\text{NS}} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{m_p}{m_e} \approx 918$. Using the numerical values: $R_{\text{WD}} \approx 4970\;\text{km}$ and $R_{\text{NS}} \approx 5\;\text{km}$, giving a ratio of about 1000. The factor of $m_p/m_e$ arises because neutrons are much heavier than electrons, so their de Broglie wavelengths are much shorter at the same momentum, leading to a much more compact degenerate object.

(b)

Conservation of angular momentum: $I_i\omega_i = I_f\omega_f$ with $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$. Since $M$ is approximately conserved:

$$R_i^2 \omega_i = R_f^2 \omega_f$$ $$P_f = P_i \frac{R_f^2}{R_i^2} = 1\;\text{day} \times \frac{(10\;\text{km})^2}{(6370\;\text{km})^2} = 86400\;\text{s} \times \frac{100}{4.06 \times 10^7}$$ $$= 86400 \times 2.46 \times 10^{-6}\;\text{s} = 0.213\;\text{s} \approx \boxed{0.2\;\text{s}}$$

This is a rotation period of about 200 ms, or about 5 rotations per second.

(c)

The moment of inertia of the neutron star:

$$I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2 = \frac{2}{5}(1.4 \times 2 \times 10^{30})(10^4)^2 = \frac{2}{5} \times 2.8 \times 10^{30} \times 10^8 = 1.12 \times 10^{38}\;\text{kg m}^2$$

The angular velocity and its derivative:

$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{P} = \frac{2\pi}{0.0335} = 187.6\;\text{rad/s}$$ $$\dot{\omega} = -\frac{2\pi\dot{P}}{P^2} = -\frac{2\pi \times 4.21 \times 10^{-13}}{(0.0335)^2} = -2.36 \times 10^{-9}\;\text{rad/s}^2$$

The spin-down luminosity:

$$L = -I\omega\dot{\omega} = 1.12 \times 10^{38} \times 187.6 \times 2.36 \times 10^{-9}$$ $$= \boxed{4.96 \times 10^{31}\;\text{W}}$$

Comparing to the sun: $L/L_\odot = 4.96 \times 10^{31} / 3.8 \times 10^{26} \approx 1.3 \times 10^5$. The Crab Pulsar's spin-down luminosity is about 100,000 times the sun's luminosity! This energy powers the Crab Nebula's glow.

(d)

The supernova occurred in 1054 AD. Taking the current year as approximately 2025, the age is $\Delta t \approx 971\;\text{yr} = 3.06 \times 10^{10}\;\text{s}$.

If $\dot{P}$ is roughly constant, the initial period was:

$$P_0 = P - \dot{P}\,\Delta t = 0.0335 - 4.21 \times 10^{-13} \times 3.06 \times 10^{10}$$ $$= 0.0335 - 0.0129 = 0.0206\;\text{s} \approx \boxed{20\;\text{ms}}$$

This initial period of about 20 ms is somewhat shorter than but of the same order as the estimate from part (b) (200 ms). The discrepancy is reasonable: the simple angular momentum conservation estimate in (b) assumed a uniform, non-differentially-rotating core and ignored mass loss during the supernova. The actual core that forms the neutron star is smaller and denser than $R_E$, and some angular momentum is carried away by the expelled material.
