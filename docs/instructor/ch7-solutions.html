Chapter 7: Ensembles — Solutions

Problem 1: Canonical Ensemble Fixed Quantity (Q1)

The answer is (b). In the canonical ensemble, $T$, $V$, and $N$ are fixed. Energy fluctuates, and the partition function $Z = \sum_k e^{-\beta E_k}$ sums over all microstates at all energies.

Problem 2: Factorization of Partition Functions (Q2)

The answer is (b). For independent subsystems with total energy $E = E_1 + E_2$, the Boltzmann factor factorizes: $e^{-\beta(E_1 + E_2)} = e^{-\beta E_1} e^{-\beta E_2}$. Therefore $Z = Z_1 \times Z_2$.

Problem 3: Chemical Potential Sign (Q3)

The answer is (c). For a classical ideal gas, $\mu = k_BT \ln(n\lambda^3)$. The classical regime requires $n\lambda^3 \ll 1$, so $\ln(n\lambda^3) < 0$, making $\mu < 0$. For air at room temperature, $n\lambda^3 \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-7}$ and $\mu \approx -0.39$ eV.

Problem 4: Boltzmann Distribution Derivation (Q4)

The answer is (b). When the system is in a definite microstate $k$ with energy $E_k$, the reservoir has energy $E_{\text{tot}} - E_k$ and can be in any of its $\Omega_{\text{res}}(E_{\text{tot}} - E_k)$ microstates. Thus $P_k \propto \Omega_{\text{res}}(E_{\text{tot}} - E_k)$. Expanding $\ln \Omega_{\text{res}}$ to first order in $E_k$ yields $P_k \propto e^{-\beta E_k}$.

Problem 5: Three-Level System (Q5)

The answer is (b). The partition function is simply the sum of Boltzmann factors over all states:

$$Z = e^{-\beta \cdot 0} + e^{-\beta \epsilon} + e^{-\beta \cdot 3\epsilon} = 1 + e^{-\beta\epsilon} + e^{-3\beta\epsilon}$$
Problem 6: Classical Harmonic Oscillator

The classical partition function is:

$$Z = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{dp}{h} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx \, e^{-\beta\left(\frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\right)}$$

The momentum integral gives $\sqrt{2\pi m / \beta}$ and the position integral gives $\sqrt{2\pi / (m\omega^2\beta)}$. Therefore:

$$Z = \frac{1}{h}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi m}{\beta}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{m\omega^2\beta}} = \frac{2\pi}{h\omega\beta} = \frac{k_BT}{\hbar\omega}$$

The average energy is:

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}\ln\left(\frac{1}{\hbar\omega\beta}\right) = \frac{1}{\beta} = k_BT$$

This matches the quantum result in the high-temperature limit. The quantum result (Eq. 35) at $k_BT \gg \hbar\omega$ gives $\langle E \rangle \approx \hbar\omega\left(\frac{k_BT}{\hbar\omega} - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\right) = k_BT$. The result also agrees with equipartition: two quadratic degrees of freedom ($p^2$ and $x^2$) each contribute $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$.

Problem 7: Energy Fluctuations

From the fluctuation-dissipation relation:

$$\langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E \rangle^2 = C_V k_B T^2 = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B \cdot k_BT^2 = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B^2T^2$$

The RMS fluctuation is $\Delta E = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}Nk_B^2T^2} = k_BT\sqrt{\frac{3N}{2}}$.

The mean energy is $\langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$. Therefore the relative fluctuation is:

$$\frac{\Delta E}{\langle E \rangle} = \frac{k_BT\sqrt{3N/2}}{\frac{3}{2}Nk_BT} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3N/2}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3N}}$$

For macroscopic $N$ (e.g. $N \sim 10^{23}$), $\frac{\Delta E}{\langle E \rangle} \sim 10^{-12}$. The energy fluctuations are negligibly small compared to the mean, so the canonical ensemble (which allows energy fluctuations) gives the same predictions as the microcanonical ensemble (which fixes energy). This is ensemble equivalence.

Problem 8: Thermal Wavelength of Helium (Q8)

The answer is (b). Using $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2\pi m k_B T}$:

$$\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}\;\text{J s}}{\sqrt{2\pi (6.65\times 10^{-27}\;\text{kg})(1.381\times 10^{-23}\;\text{J/K})(300\;\text{K})}}$$ $$= \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{3.45 \times 10^{-47}}} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{1.29 \times 10^{-23}} \approx 5.1 \times 10^{-11}\;\text{m}$$

This is about 0.051 nm. Since the interparticle spacing at STP is of order 3 nm, we have $n\lambda^3 \ll 1$, confirming helium at room temperature is safely in the classical regime.

Problem 9: Paramagnet in the Canonical Ensemble
(a)

The single-spin partition function is:

$$z_1 = e^{\beta\mu_0 B} + e^{-\beta\mu_0 B} = 2\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B)$$

Since the spins are independent, the $N$-particle partition function factorizes:

$$Z_N = (z_1)^N = [2\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B)]^N$$
(b)

The average energy is:

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z_N}{\partial \beta} = -N\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}\ln[2\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B)]$$ $$= -N\mu_0 B \tanh(\beta\mu_0 B)$$

The total magnetization is:

$$M = -\frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial B} = N\mu_0\tanh(\beta\mu_0 B)$$

At high $T$ ($\beta\mu_0 B \ll 1$): $M \approx N\mu_0^2 B/(k_BT)$, which is Curie's law. At low $T$: $M \to N\mu_0$, full alignment.

(c)

The heat capacity is:

$$C = \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial T} = -N\mu_0 B \frac{\partial}{\partial T}\tanh\left(\frac{\mu_0 B}{k_BT}\right)$$

Using $\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\tanh\left(\frac{\mu_0 B}{k_BT}\right) = \frac{1}{\cosh^2(\beta\mu_0 B)} \cdot \frac{\mu_0 B}{k_BT^2}$:

$$C = Nk_B\left(\frac{\mu_0 B}{k_BT}\right)^2 \frac{1}{\cosh^2(\beta\mu_0 B)} = Nk_B\frac{(\beta\mu_0 B)^2}{\cosh^2(\beta\mu_0 B)}$$

At low $T$, the system is frozen in its ground state so $C \to 0$. At high $T$, the two states are equally populated and adding more energy doesn't change anything, so $C \to 0$ again. Between these limits, $C$ has a maximum near $k_BT \sim \mu_0 B$. This peak is the Schottky anomaly, characteristic of systems with a finite number of energy levels.

(d)

Using $S = \frac{\langle E \rangle}{T} + k_B\ln Z$:

$$S = \frac{-N\mu_0 B\tanh(\beta\mu_0 B)}{T} + Nk_B\ln[2\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B)]$$ $$= Nk_B\left[\ln(2\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B)) - \beta\mu_0 B\tanh(\beta\mu_0 B)\right]$$

As $T \to \infty$ ($\beta \to 0$): $\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B) \to 1$ and $\tanh(\beta\mu_0 B) \to 0$, so $S \to Nk_B\ln 2$. This makes sense because at infinite temperature both spin states are equally likely, giving $\Omega = 2^N$ accessible microstates.

As $T \to 0$ ($\beta \to \infty$): $\cosh(\beta\mu_0 B) \to \frac{1}{2}e^{\beta\mu_0 B}$, $\tanh \to 1$, so $\ln(2\cosh) \to \beta\mu_0 B$ and $S \to Nk_B[\beta\mu_0 B - \beta\mu_0 B] = 0$. This makes sense because at zero temperature the system is in its unique ground state (all spins aligned), so $\Omega = 1$.

Problem 10: Diatomic Gas: Rotation and Vibration
(a)

The rotational partition function is:

$$Z_{\text{rot}} = \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}(2\ell+1)e^{-\ell(\ell+1)\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}$$

At high $T$ ($T \gg \theta_{\text{rot}}$), the spacing between levels is much smaller than $k_BT$, so the sum can be replaced by an integral. Let $x = \ell(\ell+1)$, so $dx = (2\ell+1)d\ell$:

$$Z_{\text{rot}} \approx \int_0^{\infty} (2\ell+1)\,d\ell\; e^{-\ell(\ell+1)\theta_{\text{rot}}/T} = \int_0^{\infty} dx\; e^{-x\theta_{\text{rot}}/T} = \frac{T}{\theta_{\text{rot}}}$$
(b)

Using $\langle E_{\text{rot}} \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z_{\text{rot}}}{\partial \beta}$:

$$\ln Z_{\text{rot}} = \ln\left(\frac{T}{\theta_{\text{rot}}}\right) = -\ln(\beta k_B\theta_{\text{rot}})$$ $$\langle E_{\text{rot}} \rangle = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}[-\ln(\beta k_B\theta_{\text{rot}})] = \frac{1}{\beta} = k_BT$$

The rotational contribution to heat capacity is $C_{V,\text{rot}} = \frac{\partial}{\partial T}(k_BT) = k_B$ per molecule. This is consistent with equipartition: two rotational degrees of freedom each contribute $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$.

(c)

The total heat capacity is the sum of translational ($\frac{3}{2}Nk_B$), rotational ($Nk_B$ at $T \gg \theta_{\text{rot}}$), and vibrational contributions:

$$C_V(T) = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B + Nk_B + Nk_B\left(\frac{\theta_{\text{vib}}}{T}\right)^2\frac{e^{-\theta_{\text{vib}}/T}}{(1-e^{-\theta_{\text{vib}}/T})^2}$$

At $T = 300$ K: since $300 \gg \theta_{\text{rot}} = 15.2$ K, rotation is fully excited. The vibrational factor is $(\theta_{\text{vib}}/T)^2 e^{-\theta_{\text{vib}}/T}/(1-e^{-\theta_{\text{vib}}/T})^2 = (4300/300)^2 e^{-14.3}/(1-e^{-14.3})^2 \approx 205.4 \times 6.1\times 10^{-7} \approx 1.3\times 10^{-4}$. So vibration is negligible and $C_V \approx \frac{5}{2}Nk_B$.

At $T = 6000$ K: the vibrational factor is $(4300/6000)^2 e^{-0.717}/(1-e^{-0.717})^2 = 0.513 \times 0.488/0.237 \approx 1.06$. So $C_V \approx \frac{5}{2}Nk_B + 1.06\,Nk_B \approx 3.56\,Nk_B$, approaching $\frac{7}{2}Nk_B$.

(d)

At very low $T$, only the $\ell = 0$ and $\ell = 1$ terms contribute significantly:

$$Z_{\text{rot}} \approx 1 + 3e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}$$

since $\ell = 0$ gives $e^0 = 1$ with degeneracy 1, and $\ell = 1$ gives $e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}$ with degeneracy 3.

The average rotational energy is:

$$\langle E_{\text{rot}} \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z_{\text{rot}}}{\partial \beta} = \frac{6k_B\theta_{\text{rot}}\, e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}}{1 + 3e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}}$$

For $T \ll \theta_{\text{rot}}$, the denominator is approximately 1, so:

$$\langle E_{\text{rot}} \rangle \approx 6k_B\theta_{\text{rot}}\, e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}$$ $$C_{V,\text{rot}} = \frac{\partial \langle E_{\text{rot}}\rangle}{\partial T} \approx 12k_B\left(\frac{\theta_{\text{rot}}}{T}\right)^2 e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}$$

This is exponentially suppressed by the Boltzmann factor $e^{-2\theta_{\text{rot}}/T}$, just as the vibrational heat capacity is suppressed below $\theta_{\text{vib}}$. The rotation is "frozen out" when $k_BT$ is much less than the energy gap $2k_B\theta_{\text{rot}}$ to the first excited rotational level.

Problem 11: Grand Canonical Ensemble for Adsorption Sites
(a)

A single site has two states: empty ($N=0$, $E=0$) or occupied ($N=1$, $E=-\epsilon$). The grand partition function for one site is:

$$\mathcal{Z}_1 = \sum_k e^{-\beta E_k + \beta\mu N_k} = e^{0} + e^{-\beta(-\epsilon)+\beta\mu} = 1 + e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}$$

Note: since $\epsilon > 0$ and $\mu$ is typically negative, the sign of the exponent depends on their relative magnitudes.

(b)

The $M$ sites are independent, so the full grand partition function factorizes:

$$\mathcal{Z} = (\mathcal{Z}_1)^M = [1 + e^{\beta(\epsilon + \mu)}]^M$$

The average number of adsorbed atoms is:

$$\langle N \rangle = \frac{1}{\beta}\frac{\partial \ln \mathcal{Z}}{\partial \mu} = \frac{M}{\beta} \cdot \frac{\beta e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}{1 + e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}} = \frac{M e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}{1 + e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}$$
(c)

The fractional coverage is:

$$\theta = \frac{\langle N \rangle}{M} = \frac{e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}{1+e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}$$

Dividing numerator and denominator by $e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}$:

$$\theta = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}} = \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon + \mu)} + 1} \quad \text{(wait, let's be careful)}$$

Actually, let us rewrite more carefully. We have $\theta = \frac{e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}{1+e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}}$. Dividing top and bottom by $e^{\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}$:

$$\theta = \frac{1}{e^{-\beta(\epsilon+\mu)}+1}$$

Note that since $\mu < 0$ for a classical gas and $\epsilon > 0$, the sign of $\epsilon + \mu$ depends on which dominates. This has the form of a Fermi-Dirac distribution, which makes sense: each site can hold at most one particle (like a fermion state).

(d)

Using $\mu = k_BT\ln(n\lambda^3)$ and the ideal gas law $P = nk_BT$, so $n = P/(k_BT)$:

$$e^{\beta\mu} = n\lambda^3 = \frac{P\lambda^3}{k_BT}$$

Therefore:

$$\theta = \frac{1}{e^{-\beta\epsilon}\cdot e^{-\beta\mu}+1} = \frac{1}{\frac{k_BT}{P\lambda^3}e^{-\beta\epsilon}+1}$$

Define $P_0 = \frac{k_BT}{\lambda^3}e^{-\beta\epsilon}$ (a characteristic pressure). Then:

$$\theta = \frac{P/P_0}{1+P/P_0} = \frac{P}{P + P_0}$$

At low pressure ($P \ll P_0$): $\theta \approx P/P_0 \propto P$, linear in pressure (Henry's law).

At high pressure ($P \gg P_0$): $\theta \to 1$, all sites are occupied (saturation).

This is the classic Langmuir isotherm, widely used in surface chemistry and catalysis.

Problem 12: Rubber Band Thermodynamics
(a)

The single-link partition function (right-pointing has energy 0, left-pointing has energy $\epsilon$):

$$z_1 = e^0 + e^{-\beta\epsilon} = 1 + e^{-\beta\epsilon}$$

The full partition function for $N$ independent links:

$$Z_N = (z_1)^N = (1 + e^{-\beta\epsilon})^N$$

The Helmholtz free energy is:

$$F = -k_BT\ln Z_N = -Nk_BT\ln(1 + e^{-\beta\epsilon})$$
(b)

The average number of right-pointing links is $\langle n_R \rangle = N \times P(\text{right}) = N \times \frac{1}{1+e^{-\beta\epsilon}}$. The average number of left-pointing links is $\langle n_L \rangle = N \times \frac{e^{-\beta\epsilon}}{1+e^{-\beta\epsilon}}$.

The average length is:

$$\langle L \rangle = (\langle n_R \rangle - \langle n_L \rangle)a = Na\left(\frac{1 - e^{-\beta\epsilon}}{1+e^{-\beta\epsilon}}\right) = Na\tanh\left(\frac{\beta\epsilon}{2}\right)$$
(c)

With $\epsilon = 2fa$, for small force ($\beta\epsilon = 2\beta fa \ll 1$):

$$\langle L \rangle = Na\tanh(\beta fa) \approx Na \cdot \beta fa = \frac{Na^2 f}{k_BT}$$

Actually, we need to be careful about the sign convention. If left-pointing links have higher energy $\epsilon > 0$, then without any force, the rubber band extends to maximum length $Na$ (all links right). The force $f$ should pull links to the left, meaning stretching makes the band shorter if we define length as net rightward extension.

More precisely: if an applied tension $f$ favors left-pointing links (pulling the band, giving them lower energy), then the energy of a left-pointing link is $-\epsilon = -2fa$ relative to right-pointing. In this convention, right-pointing has energy $\epsilon = 2fa$ and left-pointing has energy 0. Then:

$$\langle L \rangle = Na\left(\frac{e^{-\beta\epsilon} - 1}{e^{-\beta\epsilon}+1}\right) = -Na\tanh(\beta fa)$$

For small $\beta fa$:

$$\langle L \rangle \approx -\frac{Na^2 f}{k_BT}$$

This is Hooke's law with spring constant $k = k_BT/(Na^2)$. The spring constant increases with temperature -- the rubber band gets stiffer when heated, the opposite of a metal spring. This is because the restoring force is entropic in origin.

(d)

Using $S = \frac{\langle E\rangle}{T} + k_B\ln Z$ with the convention from part (a):

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = \frac{N\epsilon\, e^{-\beta\epsilon}}{1+e^{-\beta\epsilon}}$$ $$S = \frac{N\epsilon\, e^{-\beta\epsilon}}{T(1+e^{-\beta\epsilon})} + Nk_B\ln(1+e^{-\beta\epsilon})$$

This can be written as $S = Nk_B[-(1-p)\ln(1-p) - p\ln p]$ where $p = e^{-\beta\epsilon}/(1+e^{-\beta\epsilon})$ is the probability of a link pointing left. This is the entropy of a binary system.

When $\epsilon = 0$ (no tension): $p = 1/2$ and $S = Nk_B\ln 2$, the maximum entropy.

When $\epsilon$ is increased (stretching): $p$ decreases (fewer left-pointing links), and $S$ decreases because the configuration becomes more ordered.

For an adiabatic stretch ($dS = 0$): since stretching at constant $T$ decreases $S$, the temperature must increase to compensate and keep $S$ constant. Specifically, $S$ depends on $\epsilon$ and $T$ only through the combination $\beta\epsilon = \epsilon/(k_BT)$. So keeping $S$ constant requires keeping $\epsilon/T$ constant. If $\epsilon$ increases, $T$ must increase proportionally.

This explains why a rubber band warms up when stretched quickly (adiabatically): the entropic ordering of the polymer chains releases energy as heat. Conversely, a rubber band cools when released, as the polymer chains disorder and absorb thermal energy.
