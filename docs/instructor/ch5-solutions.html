Chapter 5: Thermodynamics — Solutions

Problem 1: Free Expansion

Answer: (b). In free expansion, no work is done ($W = 0$, since the gas pushes against vacuum) and no heat is transferred ($Q = 0$, since the walls are assumed insulating). By the first law, $\Delta E = Q + W = 0$, so the energy is unchanged. For an ideal gas, $E = C_V T$, so the temperature is also unchanged.

However, entropy increases. From Eq. (30): $\Delta S = Nk_B \ln(V_2/V_1) = Nk_B \ln 2 > 0$. The process is irreversible: restoring the original state requires work to compress the gas.

Problem 2: Heat vs. Work

Answer: (b). The chapter emphasizes (Section 1) that the distinction between heat and work is about the surroundings, not the system. The example given is a falling weight turning a pinwheel in water (work) versus heating water over a fire (heat). Both leave the system in the same final state. The key difference is whether the energy transfer mechanism involves organized motion (work) or chaotic molecular motion (heat).

Problem 3: Brownian Ratchet

Answer: (b). When $T_1 = T_2 = T$, the probability of the pawl being lifted by thermal fluctuations is the same on both sides: $P_{\text{forward}} = e^{-\epsilon/(k_BT)} = P_{\text{backward}}$. The ratchet turns forward and backward equally often, so no net work is extracted. If it could extract work from a single heat bath, it would violate the second law of thermodynamics.

Problem 4: Carnot Efficiency Calculation

Answer: (c). $\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_H - T_C}{T_H} = \frac{600 - 300}{600} = \frac{300}{600} = 0.50 = 50\%$.

Problem 5: Adiabatic Expansion Pressure

From Eq. (39), $PV^\gamma = \text{const}$, so $P_1 V_1^\gamma = P_2 V_2^\gamma$.

$$P_2 = P_1 \left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^\gamma = 3 \left(\frac{2}{6}\right)^{5/3} = 3 \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{5/3} = 3^{1} \cdot 3^{-5/3} = 3^{-2/3}$$

$$P_2 = \frac{1}{3^{2/3}} = \frac{1}{(9)^{1/3}} = \frac{1}{2.080} \approx 0.48\;\text{atm}$$

Problem 6: Isothermal Entropy Change

Answer: (b). From Eq. (30), the entropy change for a volume change at constant energy (or equivalently constant temperature for an ideal gas) is:

$$\Delta S = Nk_B \ln\frac{V_2}{V_1}$$

For one mole, $Nk_B = N_A k_B = R = 8.3\;\text{J/(K·mol)}$:

$$\Delta S = R \ln 3 = 8.3 \times 1.099 = 9.12\;\text{J/K}$$

Note that this result is independent of temperature. The entropy change depends only on the volume ratio.

Problem 7: Speed of Sound in Nitrogen

First, find $\gamma$. For a diatomic gas at room temperature, $C_V = \frac{5}{2}Nk_B$, so by Eq. (25):

$$C_P = C_V + Nk_B = \frac{7}{2}Nk_B$$

$$\gamma = \frac{C_P}{C_V} = \frac{7/2}{5/2} = \frac{7}{5} = 1.4$$

The speed of sound (from Section 3.3) is:

$$c_{\text{sound}} = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma k_B T}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{1.4 \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 300}{4.65 \times 10^{-26}}}$$

$$= \sqrt{\frac{5.796 \times 10^{-21}}{4.65 \times 10^{-26}}} = \sqrt{1.2464 \times 10^5} = 353\;\text{m/s}$$

This agrees well with the measured speed of sound in air (~343 m/s at 20°C).

Problem 8: Reservoir Entropy

Answer: (b). From Eq. (5), the entropy change of a heat reservoir is simply:

$$\Delta S_{\text{reservoir}} = \frac{Q}{T} = \frac{2000\;\text{J}}{500\;\text{K}} = 4.0\;\text{J/K}$$

A heat reservoir is defined as a system whose temperature does not change when heat flows in or out, but its entropy certainly does change. The reservoir's entropy increases when it absorbs heat.

Problem 9: Coal Power Plant
(a)

The maximum efficiency is the Carnot efficiency:

$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_H - T_C}{T_H} = \frac{840 - 300}{840} = \frac{540}{840} = \frac{9}{14} \approx 0.643$$

Maximum power output:

$$\dot{W}_{\max} = \eta_{\text{Carnot}} \cdot \dot{Q}_{\text{in}} = 0.643 \times 1500\;\text{MW} = 964\;\text{MW}$$

(b)

By energy conservation, $\dot{Q}_{\text{out}} = \dot{Q}_{\text{in}} - \dot{W}$:

$$\dot{Q}_{\text{out}} = 1500 - 964 = 536\;\text{MW}$$

(c)

The rate of heat absorption by the river equals $\dot{Q}_{\text{out}}$. The mass flow rate is $\dot{m} = \rho \dot{V} = 1000 \times 50 = 50{,}000\;\text{kg/s}$.

$$\dot{Q}_{\text{out}} = \dot{m}\,c\,\Delta T$$

$$\Delta T = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{out}}}{\dot{m}\,c} = \frac{536 \times 10^6}{50{,}000 \times 4186} = \frac{536 \times 10^6}{2.093 \times 10^8} \approx 2.6\;\text{K}$$

(d)

At 38% efficiency: $\dot{W}_{\text{real}} = 0.38 \times 1500 = 570\;\text{MW}$ and $\dot{Q}_{\text{out,real}} = 1500 - 570 = 930\;\text{MW}$.

The rate of entropy production is:

$$\dot{S}_{\text{total}} = -\frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{in}}}{T_H} + \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{out,real}}}{T_C} = -\frac{1500 \times 10^6}{840} + \frac{930 \times 10^6}{300}$$

$$= -1.786 \times 10^6 + 3.100 \times 10^6 = 1.31 \times 10^6\;\text{W/K} = 1.31\;\text{MW/K}$$

This is positive, as required by the second law. For comparison, a Carnot engine would give $\dot{S}_{\text{total}} = 0$.

Problem 10: Refrigerator
(a)

From the Carnot condition for a reversible cycle: $Q_H / T_H = Q_C / T_C$, so $Q_H = Q_C \cdot T_H / T_C$.

Energy conservation gives $W = Q_H - Q_C = Q_C\left(\frac{T_H}{T_C} - 1\right) = Q_C \cdot \frac{T_H - T_C}{T_C}$.

Therefore:

$$\text{COP}_{\max} = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}$$

(b)

$$\text{COP}_{\max} = \frac{270}{300 - 270} = \frac{270}{30} = 9$$

For every joule of work input, up to 9 joules of heat can be removed from the cold reservoir. COP can exceed 1 because a refrigerator is not converting heat to work; it is using work to move heat.

(c)

To maintain steady state, the refrigerator must remove heat at the same rate it leaks in: $\dot{Q}_C = 60\;\text{W}$.

$$\dot{W}_{\min} = \frac{\dot{Q}_C}{\text{COP}_{\max}} = \frac{60}{9} = 6.7\;\text{W}$$

(d)

With the door open, the cold and hot reservoirs are the same room. The refrigerator removes heat $Q_C$ from the room through its interior and dumps $Q_H = Q_C + W$ back into the room through its coils. The net effect is that the room gains energy $W$ from the electrical work input. The room gets warmer, not cooler. This is a direct consequence of the second law: you cannot transfer heat from cold to hot without doing work, and the work itself becomes additional heat in the room.

Problem 11: Adiabatic Atmosphere
(a)

The adiabatic relation $TP^{(1-\gamma)/\gamma} = \text{const}$ gives:

$$T(z) \cdot P(z)^{(1-\gamma)/\gamma} = T_0 \cdot P_0^{(1-\gamma)/\gamma}$$

$$T(z) = T_0 \left(\frac{P(z)}{P_0}\right)^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$$

Substituting $P(z) = P_0 e^{-z/H}$:

$$T(z) = T_0 \left(e^{-z/H}\right)^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} = T_0 \exp\!\left[-\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma}\frac{z}{H}\right]$$

(b)

For $z \ll H$, expand the exponential to first order: $e^{-x} \approx 1 - x$.

$$T(z) \approx T_0\left(1 - \frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma}\frac{z}{H}\right)$$

Taking the derivative:

$$\frac{dT}{dz} = -\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma}\frac{T_0}{H}$$

(c)

With $\gamma = 7/5$, $T_0 = 300\;\text{K}$, and $H = 8.5\;\text{km}$:

$$\frac{dT}{dz} = -\frac{2/5}{7/5}\frac{300}{8.5} = -\frac{2}{7}\frac{300}{8.5} = -\frac{600}{59.5} \approx -10.1\;\text{K/km}$$

The observed average lapse rate in the troposphere is about $6.5\;\text{K/km}$, which is significantly less than the adiabatic prediction of $10.1\;\text{K/km}$.

(d)

The adiabatic lapse rate overestimates the actual temperature drop because it ignores the latent heat released when water vapor condenses as air rises and cools. As moist air rises, water condenses and releases latent heat, partially warming the air parcel and reducing the rate of temperature decrease. This effect is captured in the "moist adiabatic lapse rate," which is typically $5$–$6\;\text{K/km}$, closer to the observed average. The actual tropospheric lapse rate falls between the dry and moist adiabatic values because the atmosphere is only partially saturated.

Problem 12: Otto Cycle
(a)

For an adiabatic process, $TV^{\gamma-1} = \text{const}$.

Stage 1 (adiabatic compression, $V_1 \to V_2$):

$$T_1 V_1^{\gamma-1} = T_2 V_2^{\gamma-1} \implies T_2 = T_1\left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^{\gamma-1} = T_1\,r^{\gamma-1}$$

Stage 3 (adiabatic expansion, $V_2 \to V_1$):

$$T_3 V_2^{\gamma-1} = T_4 V_1^{\gamma-1} \implies T_4 = T_3\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)^{\gamma-1} = \frac{T_3}{r^{\gamma-1}}$$

(b)

The efficiency is:

$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_{\text{in}}} = \frac{Q_{\text{in}} - Q_{\text{out}}}{Q_{\text{in}}} = 1 - \frac{Q_{\text{out}}}{Q_{\text{in}}} = 1 - \frac{C_V(T_4 - T_1)}{C_V(T_3 - T_2)}$$

Substituting $T_2 = T_1 r^{\gamma-1}$ and $T_4 = T_3/r^{\gamma-1}$:

$$\eta = 1 - \frac{T_3/r^{\gamma-1} - T_1}{T_3 - T_1 r^{\gamma-1}} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}} \cdot \frac{T_3 - T_1 r^{\gamma-1}}{T_3 - T_1 r^{\gamma-1}}$$

$$\boxed{\eta_{\text{Otto}} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}}$$

(c)

With $r = 10$ and $\gamma = 1.4$:

$$\eta_{\text{Otto}} = 1 - \frac{1}{10^{0.4}} = 1 - \frac{1}{2.512} = 1 - 0.398 = 0.602 \approx 60.2\%$$

The Carnot efficiency between $T_1 = 300\;\text{K}$ and $T_3 = 2500\;\text{K}$:

$$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} = 1 - \frac{300}{2500} = 1 - 0.12 = 88\%$$

The Otto efficiency (60%) is significantly less than the Carnot efficiency (88%).

(d)

The Otto efficiency is less than the Carnot efficiency because the Otto cycle does not operate between just two temperatures. In the isochoric (constant volume) stages, the gas temperature changes continuously, meaning heat is absorbed and rejected over a range of temperatures rather than at fixed $T_H$ and $T_C$. This is thermodynamically less efficient than the Carnot cycle, which exchanges heat only at the extreme temperatures.

The Otto cycle is reversible in the idealized form presented here: both adiabatic stages are isentropic, and the isochoric stages can in principle be carried out reversibly by contact with a series of reservoirs at incrementally different temperatures. However, the real Otto cycle in a car engine is highly irreversible due to friction, turbulence, and the rapid combustion process.
