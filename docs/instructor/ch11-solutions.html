Chapter 11: Phonons & Photons — Solutions

Problem 1: Chemical Potential of Phonons and Photons (Q1)

Answer: (b). Phonons and photons can be freely created and destroyed — processes like $\text{atom} \to \text{atom} + \text{phonon}$ are allowed. In equilibrium, the chemical potentials must balance across any reaction. Since the atom's chemical potential is unchanged, $\mu_\text{phonon} = 0$. The same argument applies to photons. This is not a property of all bosons (e.g., atoms undergoing BEC have $\mu \neq 0$ because atom number is conserved), nor is it required for the partition function to converge.

Problem 2: Debye Cutoff Frequency (Q2)

Answer: (b). A solid with $N$ atoms has $3N$ degrees of freedom (each atom can move in 3 directions), so there are exactly $3N$ independent normal modes. The Debye cutoff $\omega_D$ is fixed by requiring $\int_0^{\omega_D} g(\omega)\,d\omega = 3N$. Using $g(\omega) = \frac{3V}{2\pi^2 c_s^3}\omega^2$, this gives $\frac{V}{2\pi^2 c_s^3}\omega_D^3 = 3N$, or $\omega_D = c_s\left(\frac{6\pi^2 N}{V}\right)^{1/3}$.

Problem 3: Ultraviolet Catastrophe (Q3)

Answer: (c). The classical Rayleigh-Jeans law assigns energy $k_BT$ to each mode via equipartition, giving $I(\omega) \propto \omega^2 k_BT$, which diverges at high frequency. Planck's quantization $\epsilon = \hbar\omega$ means that modes with $\hbar\omega \gg k_BT$ are exponentially suppressed by the Bose-Einstein factor $\frac{1}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1} \approx e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}$. This suppression cuts off the spectrum at high frequencies, giving a finite total energy.

Problem 4: Photon vs. Phonon Density of States (Q4)

Answer: (a). The phonon density of states is $g(\omega) = \frac{3V}{2\pi^2 c_s^3}\omega^2$ (factor of 3 for polarizations, speed $c_s$), while the photon density of states is $g(\omega) = \frac{V}{\pi^2 c^3}\omega^2 = \frac{2V}{2\pi^2 c^3}\omega^2$ (factor of 2 for polarizations, speed $c$). The two key differences in the formula itself are: (1) 2 polarizations for photons vs. 3 for phonons (photons lack the longitudinal polarization because $\vec{k}\cdot\vec{E}_0 = 0$), and (2) $c$ replaces $c_s$. There is also the absence of an upper cutoff for photons, but this is a difference in the integration limits, not the density of states function.

Problem 5: Diamond vs. Lead Heat Capacity (Q5)

For lead, $T/T_D = 300/105 = 2.86 \gg 1$, so we are well in the high-temperature regime. Thus $C_V \approx 3R = 24.9\,\text{J/(mol·K)}$ (Dulong-Petit). Lead's heat capacity is very close to the classical value.

For diamond, $T/T_D = 300/2230 = 0.134 \ll 1$, so we are in the low-temperature regime. Using Eq. (24):

$$C_V = \frac{12\pi^4}{5}R\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3 = \frac{12\pi^4}{5}(8.314)\left(\frac{300}{2230}\right)^3$$ $$= \frac{12\pi^4}{5}(8.314)(0.134)^3 = (233.8)(8.314)(2.41 \times 10^{-3}) = 4.69\,\text{J/(mol·K)}$$

Diamond's heat capacity is far below the Dulong-Petit value because most phonon modes are frozen out at room temperature (quantum regime). Lead is much closer to the classical value because its heavy atoms and weak bonds give a low Debye temperature.

Problem 6: Star Temperature from Wien's Law (Q6)

From Wien's displacement law (Eq. 65):

$$T = \frac{2.89\,\text{mm}\cdot\text{K}}{\lambda_\text{peak}} = \frac{2.89 \times 10^{-3}}{960 \times 10^{-9}} = 3010\,\text{K}$$

This is a cool star. Its peak is in the near-infrared, but it still emits significant visible light. The visible portion of its spectrum is dominated by red wavelengths, so the star would appear red-orange to the eye (similar to Betelgeuse).

The total radiative flux from the Stefan-Boltzmann law:

$$\Phi = \sigma T^4 = (5.67 \times 10^{-8})(3010)^4 = (5.67 \times 10^{-8})(8.21 \times 10^{13}) = 4.65 \times 10^6\,\text{W/m}^2$$
Problem 7: Blackbody Cavity at 3000 K (Q7)
(a)

Using $E/V = (4\sigma/c)T^4$ (Eq. 58):

$$E = \frac{4\sigma}{c}T^4 V = \frac{4(5.67 \times 10^{-8})}{3 \times 10^8}(3000)^4(1)$$ $$= (7.56 \times 10^{-16})(8.1 \times 10^{13}) = 0.0612\,\text{J}$$
(b)

Using Eq. (73), $\langle N \rangle = 16\pi V\left(\frac{k_BT}{hc}\right)^3 \zeta(3)$:

$$\frac{k_BT}{hc} = \frac{(1.381 \times 10^{-23})(3000)}{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)} = \frac{4.143 \times 10^{-20}}{1.988 \times 10^{-25}} = 2.085 \times 10^5\,\text{m}^{-1}$$ $$\langle N \rangle = 16\pi(1)(2.085 \times 10^5)^3(1.202) = 16\pi(9.07 \times 10^{15})(1.202) = 5.48 \times 10^{17}$$
(c)

Average energy per photon:

$$\frac{E}{N} = \frac{0.0612}{5.48 \times 10^{17}} = 1.12 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{J} = 0.70\,\text{eV}$$

This is in the near-infrared, consistent with Wien's law giving $\lambda_\text{peak} \approx 960\,\text{nm}$ at 3000 K.

Problem 8: Low-Temperature Entropy of Debye Model (Q8)

The low-temperature heat capacity is $C_V = \frac{12\pi^4}{5}Nk_B\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3$ (Eq. 24). The entropy is:

$$S = \int_0^T \frac{C_V}{T'}\,dT' = \frac{12\pi^4}{5}Nk_B \frac{1}{T_D^3}\int_0^T T'^2\,dT' = \frac{12\pi^4}{5}Nk_B\frac{T^3}{3T_D^3} = \frac{4\pi^4}{5}Nk_B\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3$$

Note that $S = C_V/3$ (not $4C_V/3$; the problem statement should read $S = C_V/3$). As $T \to 0$, $S \propto T^3 \to 0$, consistent with the third law.

The entropy per atom equals $k_B$ when:

$$\frac{4\pi^4}{5}\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3 = 1 \implies \frac{T}{T_D} = \left(\frac{5}{4\pi^4}\right)^{1/3} = \left(\frac{5}{389.6}\right)^{1/3} = (0.01284)^{1/3} = 0.234$$ $$T = 0.234\,T_D$$

For example, for copper ($T_D = 315\,\text{K}$), this gives $T \approx 74\,\text{K}$.

Problem 9: The Debye Model in Detail
(a)

Starting from Eq. (20) with $\omega_D = k_BT_D/\hbar$, substitute $x = \beta\hbar\omega$, so $\omega = x/(\beta\hbar) = xk_BT/\hbar$ and $d\omega = dx/(\beta\hbar)$. The upper limit becomes $\beta\hbar\omega_D = T_D/T$.

$$E = \frac{9N\hbar}{\omega_D^3}\int_0^{\omega_D}\frac{\omega^3}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1}\,d\omega = \frac{9N\hbar}{\omega_D^3}\frac{1}{(\beta\hbar)^4}\int_0^{T_D/T}\frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}\,dx$$ $$= \frac{9N\hbar}{\omega_D^3}\frac{(k_BT)^4}{\hbar^4}\int_0^{T_D/T}\frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}\,dx = 9Nk_BT\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3\int_0^{T_D/T}\frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}\,dx$$

where we used $\hbar\omega_D = k_BT_D$. Defining $f_D(y) = \frac{3}{y^3}\int_0^{y}\frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}\,dx$ with $y = T_D/T$:

$$E = 3Nk_BT\,f_D(T_D/T)$$
(b)

For $T \gg T_D$, $y = T_D/T \ll 1$, so $x \ll 1$ throughout the integration range. Expanding $e^x - 1 \approx x$:

$$f_D(y) \approx \frac{3}{y^3}\int_0^y \frac{x^3}{x}\,dx = \frac{3}{y^3}\int_0^y x^2\,dx = \frac{3}{y^3}\frac{y^3}{3} = 1$$

Therefore $E = 3Nk_BT$, giving $C_V = 3Nk_B$, which is the Dulong-Petit law.

(c)

For $T \ll T_D$, $y = T_D/T \to \infty$. The integrand $\frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}$ decays exponentially for large $x$, so extending the upper limit to $\infty$ introduces negligible error:

$$E \approx 9Nk_BT\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3\int_0^{\infty}\frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}\,dx = 9Nk_BT\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3\frac{\pi^4}{15}$$ $$= \frac{3\pi^4}{5}Nk_BT\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3$$

Taking the derivative:

$$C_V = \frac{\partial E}{\partial T} = \frac{3\pi^4}{5}Nk_B \cdot 4\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3 = \frac{12\pi^4}{5}Nk_B\left(\frac{T}{T_D}\right)^3$$
(d)

In the Einstein model, all $3N$ oscillators have the same frequency $\omega_E$. The average energy of one oscillator is $\langle\epsilon\rangle = \frac{\hbar\omega_E}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega_E} - 1}$, so the total energy is:

$$E = \frac{3N\hbar\omega_E}{e^{\hbar\omega_E/(k_BT)} - 1}$$

Defining $\theta_E = \hbar\omega_E/k_B$ and differentiating:

$$C_V = \frac{\partial E}{\partial T} = 3Nk_B\left(\frac{\theta_E}{T}\right)^2\frac{e^{\theta_E/T}}{(e^{\theta_E/T} - 1)^2}$$

As $T \to 0$, $\theta_E/T \to \infty$ and:

$$C_V \approx 3Nk_B\left(\frac{\theta_E}{T}\right)^2 e^{-\theta_E/T}$$

This vanishes exponentially as $T \to 0$, much faster than the Debye $T^3$ law. The Debye model is more physical at low $T$ because it includes low-frequency modes that are easily excited even at low temperatures. The Einstein model has a frequency gap: no modes below $\omega_E$, so the heat capacity is exponentially suppressed. In reality, solids always have acoustic phonon modes with arbitrarily low frequencies (for long wavelengths), so the Debye $T^3$ behavior is observed experimentally.

Problem 10: Blackbody Thermodynamics
(a)

From $\ln\mathcal{Z} = \frac{\pi^2 V}{45 c^3 \hbar^3}\left(\frac{1}{\beta}\right)^3 = \frac{\pi^2 V}{45 c^3 \hbar^3}(k_BT)^3$:

$$F = -k_BT\ln\mathcal{Z} = -\frac{\pi^2 V k_B^4}{45 c^3 \hbar^3}T^4 = -\frac{4\sigma V}{3c}T^4$$

Entropy:

$$S = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\bigg|_V = \frac{16\sigma V}{3c}T^3$$

Pressure:

$$P = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\bigg|_T = \frac{4\sigma}{3c}T^4$$

Checking: $E = F + TS = -\frac{4\sigma V}{3c}T^4 + T\frac{16\sigma V}{3c}T^3 = \frac{12\sigma V}{3c}T^4 = \frac{4\sigma V}{c}T^4$.

And $3PV = 3\frac{4\sigma}{3c}T^4 V = \frac{4\sigma V}{c}T^4 = E$. Verified.

(b)

The entropy $S = \frac{16\sigma}{3c}T^3 V$ is constant in an adiabatic process, so $T^3 V = \text{const}$, giving:

$$T = T_1\left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^{1/3}$$

If the volume doubles:

$$T_2 = 6000 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{1/3} = 6000 \times 0.794 = 4762\,\text{K}$$
(c)

The pressure $P = \frac{4\sigma}{3c}T^4$ is independent of volume at fixed $T$. Therefore:

$$\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial P}\right)_T \to \infty$$

and $\kappa_T = -\frac{1}{V}\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial P}\right)_T = \infty$. The photon gas is infinitely compressible isothermally! This is because the pressure depends only on temperature, not on volume. Compressing the gas at constant $T$ does not change the pressure — photons are simply absorbed or emitted by the walls to maintain equilibrium.

For an ideal gas, $P = Nk_BT/V$, so $\kappa_T = 1/P$, which is finite. The ideal gas has a fixed number of particles, while the photon gas freely adjusts its number.

(d)

At constant pressure, $P = \frac{4\sigma}{3c}T^4 = \text{const}$ means $T = \text{const}$. But then adding heat at constant $T$ means all the heat goes into expanding the volume (creating more photons), not into raising the temperature. The ratio $C_P = \frac{dQ}{dT}\big|_P$ is infinite because $dT = 0$ for any finite $dQ$.

More formally, since $P$ depends only on $T$, constant $P$ implies constant $T$, so the system cannot change temperature at constant pressure. Any heat added at constant $P$ goes entirely into increasing the volume (and creating new photons), giving $C_P = \infty$.

Problem 11: The Cosmic Microwave Background
(a)

From Wien's displacement law:

$$\lambda_\text{peak} = \frac{2.89\,\text{mm}\cdot\text{K}}{2.725\,\text{K}} = 1.061\,\text{mm}$$

The peak frequency: $\nu_\text{peak} = c/\lambda_\text{peak} = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{1.061 \times 10^{-3}} = 2.83 \times 10^{11}\,\text{Hz} = 283\,\text{GHz}$.

Note: more precisely, the peak in frequency space occurs at a different value because the Jacobian changes the peak location. Using $\nu_\text{peak} = 2.821\,k_BT/h$: $\nu_\text{peak} = 2.821 \times \frac{(1.381\times 10^{-23})(2.725)}{6.626\times 10^{-34}} = 160\,\text{GHz}$. The CMB peaks in the microwave region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

(b)

Energy density (Eq. 58):

$$\frac{E}{V} = \frac{4\sigma}{c}T^4 = \frac{4(5.67 \times 10^{-8})}{3 \times 10^8}(2.725)^4 = (7.56 \times 10^{-16})(55.1) = 4.17 \times 10^{-14}\,\text{J/m}^3$$ $$= \frac{4.17 \times 10^{-14}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \times 10^{-6}\,\text{eV/cm}^3 = 0.261\,\text{eV/cm}^3 \approx 0.26\,\text{MeV/m}^3$$

Photon number density (Eq. 73):

$$\frac{N}{V} = 16\pi\left(\frac{k_BT}{hc}\right)^3\zeta(3)$$ $$\frac{k_BT}{hc} = \frac{(1.381\times 10^{-23})(2.725)}{(6.626\times 10^{-34})(3\times 10^8)} = 1.893 \times 10^2\,\text{m}^{-1}$$ $$\frac{N}{V} = 16\pi(189.3)^3(1.202) = 16\pi(6.785 \times 10^6)(1.202) = 4.10 \times 10^8\,\text{m}^{-3}$$

Entropy density (Eq. 74): $S/V = 3.61\,k_B\,(N/V) = 3.61(1.381 \times 10^{-23})(4.10 \times 10^8) = 2.04 \times 10^{-14}\,\text{J/(K·m}^3\text{)}$, or equivalently $S/(Vk_B) = 1.48 \times 10^9\,\text{m}^{-3}$.

(c)

Volume of observable universe: $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi(4.4 \times 10^{26})^3 = 3.57 \times 10^{80}\,\text{m}^3$.

Total CMB photons: $N = (4.10 \times 10^8)(3.57 \times 10^{80}) = 1.46 \times 10^{89}$.

Total entropy: $S = 3.61\,k_B N = 3.61(1.46 \times 10^{89})k_B = 5.3 \times 10^{89}\,k_B \approx 10^{90}\,k_B$.

This is the dominant source of entropy in the observable universe.

(d)

The scale factor at recombination relative to today: $a_\text{rec}/a_0 = T_0/T_\text{rec} = 2.725/3000 = 9.08 \times 10^{-4}$. The universe has expanded by a factor of $a_0/a_\text{rec} = 3000/2.725 \approx 1100$.

The entropy is $S = \frac{16\sigma}{3c}T^3 V$. Since $T \propto 1/a$ and $V \propto a^3$:

$$S \propto T^3 V \propto \frac{1}{a^3}\cdot a^3 = \text{const}$$

The entropy is conserved even though $T$ decreases, because the volume increases by exactly the right factor. This is an adiabatic expansion — the CMB photons do not exchange heat with anything, so entropy is conserved. The number of photons $N \propto T^3 V \propto \text{const}$ is also conserved.

Problem 12: Thermal Radiation and Planetary Temperatures
(a)

The power intercepted by the planet from the Sun is:

$$P_\text{in} = (1-\alpha)\,\pi R_p^2\,\frac{L_\odot}{4\pi d^2}$$

where $L_\odot = 4\pi R_\odot^2 \sigma T_\odot^4$ is the solar luminosity. So:

$$P_\text{in} = (1-\alpha)\pi R_p^2\frac{R_\odot^2 \sigma T_\odot^4}{d^2}$$

The planet re-radiates as a blackbody over its full surface:

$$P_\text{out} = 4\pi R_p^2 \sigma T_p^4$$

Setting $P_\text{in} = P_\text{out}$:

$$(1-\alpha)\frac{R_\odot^2 T_\odot^4}{4d^2} = T_p^4$$ $$T_p = T_\odot\left(\frac{R_\odot}{2d}\right)^{1/2}(1-\alpha)^{1/4}$$
(b)

For Earth:

$$T_p = 5780\sqrt{\frac{6.96 \times 10^8}{2(1.50 \times 10^{11})}}(1-0.30)^{1/4} = 5780\sqrt{2.32 \times 10^{-3}}(0.70)^{1/4}$$ $$= 5780(0.04817)(0.915) = 255\,\text{K}$$

For Mars:

$$T_p = 5780\sqrt{\frac{6.96 \times 10^8}{2(2.28 \times 10^{11})}}(0.75)^{1/4} = 5780(0.03906)(0.930) = 210\,\text{K}$$

Mars: the prediction of $\sim 210\,\text{K}$ agrees well with the observed $\sim 210\,\text{K}$ because Mars has a very thin atmosphere with negligible greenhouse effect.

Earth: the prediction of $\sim 255\,\text{K}$ is $33\,\text{K}$ below the actual average of $288\,\text{K}$. The discrepancy is due to Earth's greenhouse effect — the atmosphere (primarily H$_2$O and CO$_2$) absorbs and re-emits infrared radiation, warming the surface above the bare equilibrium temperature.

(c)

In the single-layer greenhouse model, let $F_s$ be the solar flux absorbed by the ground (same as in part (a): $F_s = \sigma T_p^4$ where $T_p$ is the bare equilibrium temperature). The atmosphere is transparent to visible (solar) light but absorbs all infrared (ground) emission.

Let $T_g$ be the ground temperature and $T_a$ the atmospheric temperature. Energy balance for the atmosphere (absorbs ground IR, emits up and down):

$$\sigma T_g^4 = 2\sigma T_a^4 \implies T_a = \frac{T_g}{2^{1/4}}$$

Energy balance for the ground (absorbs solar + downward atmospheric IR):

$$F_s + \sigma T_a^4 = \sigma T_g^4$$ $$\sigma T_p^4 + \sigma T_a^4 = \sigma T_g^4$$

Substituting $T_a^4 = T_g^4/2$:

$$T_p^4 = T_g^4 - T_g^4/2 = T_g^4/2$$ $$T_g = 2^{1/4}\,T_p$$

For Earth: $T_g = 2^{1/4}(255) = 1.189(255) = 303\,\text{K}$.

This is reasonably close to the actual 288 K. The simple one-layer model overestimates slightly because the real atmosphere is not perfectly opaque to all infrared wavelengths.

(d)

The Sun's peak wavelength (at 5780 K):

$$\lambda_\text{peak,Sun} = \frac{2.89\,\text{mm}\cdot\text{K}}{5780\,\text{K}} = 500\,\text{nm} \quad\text{(visible, green-yellow)}$$

Earth's peak wavelength (at 288 K):

$$\lambda_\text{peak,Earth} = \frac{2.89\,\text{mm}\cdot\text{K}}{288\,\text{K}} = 10.0\,\mu\text{m} \quad\text{(mid-infrared)}$$

Earth's thermal emission peaks in the mid-infrared, about 20 times longer in wavelength than the Sun's emission. The greenhouse effect works because the atmosphere is largely transparent to visible light (letting solar radiation through) but absorbs strongly in the infrared (trapping Earth's thermal emission). Greenhouse gases like CO$_2$ and H$_2$O have strong absorption bands in the infrared, particularly around $10$–$15\,\mu\text{m}$, right where Earth's emission peaks.
