Chapter 8

Free Energy

8.1 Introduction

The terms on the right of $dE = TdS - PdV + \mu dN$ are different ways the energy can change: the first term is when heat comes in, since $dS = dQ/T$, the second term is work going out from expanding volume, the third term is the energy associated with bond formation or increasing particle creation.

Holding any two of the four differentials fixed we can derive from this equation lots of other formulas

$$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\right)_{V,N} = \frac{1}{T};\qquad \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial N}\right)_{E,V} = -\frac{\mu}{T};\qquad \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_{E,N} = \frac{P}{T}$$ (1)
$$\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial S}\right)_{V,N} = T;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial V}\right)_{S,N} = -P$$ (2)

and so on. Some of these are more useful than others. It's important to label what you are holding fixed because $S$, $E$, $V$, $N$ are not all independent. Any one of these variables is a function of the others:

$$E = E(S, V, N);\qquad S = S(E, V, N);\qquad V = V(E, S, N);\qquad N = N(E, S, V)$$ (3)

The variables $T$, $P$, $\mu$ are then derived quantities, calculated by taking partial derivatives with respect to the independent variables.

We often use monatomic ideal gases to check these general relations. For example, the entropy of a monatomic ideal gas is

$$S(E, V, N) = Nk_B\left[\ln\frac{V}{N} + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{4\pi mE}{3Nh^2}\right) + \frac{5}{2}\right]$$ (4)

Then $\frac{1}{T} = \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\right)_{V,N} = \frac{3}{2}\frac{Nk_B}{E}$, consistent with $E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$. Similarly, $P = T\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_{E,N} = \frac{Nk_BT}{V}$, which is the ideal gas law.

Since partial derivatives commute, we can derive additional relations:

$$\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_{S,N} = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_{V,N}$$ (5)

and so on. These type of relations among derivatives are known as Maxwell relationsRelations between second partial derivatives of thermodynamic potentials that follow from the commutativity of mixed partial derivatives.

When we have a function like $S(V, E, N)$, we can see how the system responds when we change $V$, $E$ and $N$. In physical situations, we are often much more interested in knowing how our system responds to changes in temperature or pressure. So we would like the system to be described by functions that depend explicitly on $T$ and $P$ rather than $E$ and $V$. In this lecture, we construct new variables (free energies) that depend on $T$ and $P$. The four new potentials we introduce are

Thermodynamic Potentials

Helmholtz free energy: $F \equiv E - TS$

Enthalpy: $H \equiv E + PV$

Gibbs free energy: $G \equiv E + PV - TS$

Grand free energy: $\Omega \equiv E - TS - \mu N$

These are general definitions of these new variables, holding for arbitrary systems.

As a warning, this business of dependent and independent variables is going to feel a bit awkward at first. That is because we are used to having a clear distinction between independent and dependent variables, while in statistical mechanics it is very handy to mix up the dependent and independent variables depending on context. Although awkward at first, this approach is very powerful and worth mastering.

8.2 Euler Identity

Before getting started with all the new potentials, there is actually a very nice relation we can derive among all the different interdependent variables using only $dE = TdS - PdV + \mu dN$ and the fact that entropy is extensive.

$E$, $V$, $N$ and $S$ are all extensive quantities. When we double the size of a system, they all double. This is in contrast to $P$, $\mu$ and $T$ which are intensive quantities.

It's actually very hard to make an extensive function. Any extensive function $f(x)$ must satisfy $f(cx) = cf(x)$ for any $c$. Differentiating with respect to $c$ gives $xf'(cx) = f(x)$. Setting $c = 1$ gives $xf' = f$, so $f(x) = ax$ for some $a$: $f$ must be a linear function.

▶ Derivation: Euler Equation
Step 1
Extensivity requires $S(cE, cV, cN) = cS(E, V, N)$.
Step 2
Differentiating both sides with respect to $c$: $$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial(cE)}\right)_{V,N} E + \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial(cV)}\right)_{E,N} V + \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial(cN)}\right)_{V,E} N = S(E, V, N)$$
Step 3
Setting $c = 1$ and using the definitions of $T$, $P$, $\mu$: $$S = \frac{E}{T} + \frac{P}{T}V - \frac{\mu}{T}N$$

This gives the Euler equation$E = TS - PV + \mu N$: a fundamental relation linking all thermodynamic variables, valid when entropy is extensive:

$$E = TS - PV + \mu N$$ (6)

A related result comes from taking the total derivative of both sides and subtracting $dE = TdS - PdV + \mu dN$:

$$SdT - VdP + Nd\mu = 0$$ (7)

This is called the Gibbs-Duhem equation$SdT - VdP + Nd\mu = 0$: it says that $T$, $P$ and $\mu$ are not independent -- changing $T$ and $P$ makes $\mu$ change in a certain way.. It says that $T$, $P$ and $\mu$ are not independent -- changing $T$ and $P$ makes $\mu$ change in a certain way.

8.3 Helmholtz Free Energy

We define the Helmholtz free energy$F = E - TS$: the energy available to do work at constant temperature and volume as

$$F \equiv E - TS$$ (8)

Free energy is a concept particularly useful at constant temperature. We can take the differential of $F$:

$$dF = dE - TdS - SdT = -PdV + \mu dN - SdT$$ (9)

Note that the $dS$ has dropped out. The right-hand side suggests that we should think of $F$ as a function of $V$, $N$ and $T$: $F = F(V, N, T)$. We say that $V$, $N$, $T$ are the natural arguments of the Helmholtz free energy. We can then generate Maxwell relations:

$$\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_{N,T} = -P;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial N}\right)_{V,T} = \mu;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_{V,N} = -S$$ (10)

It is worth pointing out that the operation of replacing the dependent variable $S$ in $E(S, V, N)$ with a new dependent variable given by a derivative, $T = \frac{\partial E}{\partial S}$, is known as a Legendre transformA mathematical operation that trades a variable for its conjugate derivative, e.g. going from $E(S,V,N)$ to $F(T,V,N) = E - TS$.

8.3.1 Free Energy for Work

The Helmholtz free energy is one of the most useful quantities in thermodynamics. Its usefulness stems from the fact that $dV$, $dN$ and $dT$ are readily measurable. Free energy is particularly powerful for systems at constant temperature where $dF = dE - TdS$. In previous courses you have studied mechanical systems using energy. Mechanical systems all have fixed degrees of freedom, so $S = 0$ and $F = E$. Free energy is a generalization of energy whose importance is revealed by working at finite $T$.

Consider an isolated system in volume $V$ kept at constant $T$ by being in contact with a heat bath. The change in free energy satisfies:

$$W \leq -\Delta F_{\text{system}}$$ (11)

where the inequality becomes an equality if and only if the transformation is done reversibly. So the free energy of the system is depleted to do the work. This is why free energy is called free: it is the energy available to do work.

When $W = 0$, we get $\Delta F_{\text{system}} \leq 0$. Thus:

Key Properties of Helmholtz Free Energy
  • Free energy is to a constant $T$ system what $E$ is to a mechanical system.
  • Helmholtz free energy is the available energy to do work at constant $T$ and $V$.
  • In a system kept at constant $T$ and $V$, interacting with the surroundings only through an exchange of heat (i.e. no work), the Helmholtz free energy never increases.
  • In an isolated system at constant $T$ and $V$, Helmholtz free energy is minimized in equilibrium.
  • Free energy refers to the free energy of the system only, $F = F_{\text{system}}$.

Another important point to keep in mind is that free energy, like entropy, is a property of the ensemble. You cannot talk about the free energy or entropy of an individual microstate.

8.3.2 Free Energy and the Partition Function

Recall that in the canonical ensemble, $S = \frac{\langle E \rangle}{T} + k_B\ln Z$. So for an isolated system where $\langle E \rangle = E$ we immediately get that

$$F = -k_BT \ln Z$$ (12)

Equivalently, $e^{-\beta F} = Z = \sum e^{-\beta E}$, which shows that the free energy is the same as the energy if there is only one microstate.

For a monatomic ideal gas:

$$F = -Nk_BT\left(\ln\frac{V}{N} + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{2\pi mk_BT}{h^2}\right) + 1\right)$$ (13)

One can verify that $\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_{V,N} = -S$ reproduces the Sackur-Tetrode formula, and $P = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_{T,N} = \frac{Nk_BT}{V}$ gives the ideal gas law.

8.3.3 Spring and Gas

To build some more intuition for free energy, let's consider some examples. First, consider the free expansion of a gas from $V_1 \to V_2$. No work is done. The internal energy doesn't change, $\Delta E = 0$. The entropy change is $\Delta S = Nk_B \ln \frac{V_2}{V_1}$. So the change in free energy is

$$\Delta F = \Delta E - T\Delta S = -Nk_BT\ln\frac{V_2}{V_1}$$ (14)

This transition is completely driven by entropic considerations.

Interactive 1: Spring-Piston Equilibrium

Spring-loaded piston against gas in a heat bath. Equilibrium occurs where Helmholtz free energy is minimized.

Now consider a piston on a spring, with force constant $k$, immersed in a heat bath. Let $x$ be the displacement of the piston from its equilibrium position, with energy $E(x, \dot{x}) = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2$. The entropy of the spring is zero. Since $S = 0$, $F = E$. Minimizing $F$ corresponds to minimizing $E$, giving equilibrium at $x = \dot{x} = 0$.

Now put the spring and the gas together. Setting $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = 0$ gives

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = \mathcal{F}_{\text{piston}} - \mathcal{F}_{\text{gas}}$$ (15)

Setting the variation to zero implies $\mathcal{F}_{\text{piston}} = \mathcal{F}_{\text{gas}}$: forces are equal at equilibrium. We can find the equilibrium by minimizing the free energy -- the result is the same as demanding that forces balance.

8.3.4 Energy (Non)-Minimization

It is common lore to think of energy being minimized: a ball rolls down to the bottom of a hill and stays there. But energy is conserved, so where does this come from? It comes from free energy! All those systems in which energy is minimized are really minimizing free energy.

Energy Minimization is Really Entropy Maximization
$$\Delta F = \Delta E - T\Delta S = -T\left[\underbrace{\frac{-\Delta E}{T}}_{\Delta S_{\text{surr}}} + \underbrace{\Delta S}_{\Delta S_{\text{sys}}}\right]$$

The tendency to minimize free energy is entirely because of a tendency to maximize entropy. In summary, energy minimization is really free energy minimization, which really is entropy maximization.

At low T, energy wins and the ordered state is favored. At high T, entropy wins and the disordered state has lower F.

8.4 Enthalpy

We use the symbol $H$ for enthalpy$H = E + PV$: the thermodynamic potential useful at constant pressure. The heat capacity at constant pressure is $C_P = (\partial H/\partial T)_P$.. It is defined as

$$H \equiv E + PV$$ (16)

So, using $dE = TdS - PdV + \mu dN$:

$$dH = TdS + VdP + \mu dN$$ (17)

Thus

$$\left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial S}\right)_{P,N} = T;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial P}\right)_{S,N} = V;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial N}\right)_{P,S} = \mu$$ (18)

Enthalpy is useful at constant pressure. It is a function $H = H(S, P, N)$.

Recall the two heat capacities: $C_V = \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial T}\right)_V$ and $C_P = \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial T}\right)_P$. For constant $V$, $C_V = \left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial T}\right)_V$. For constant $P$, the total energy change is $\Delta E = Q - P\Delta V$, so $\Delta H = Q$. Thus:

$$C_P = \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial T}\right)_P$$ (19)

Thus enthalpy plays the role at constant pressure that energy does at constant volume.

For a monatomic ideal gas, $H = E + PV = \frac{5}{2}Nk_BT$, so $C_P = \frac{5}{2}Nk_B$.

8.4.1 Enthalpy of Chemical Bonds

Enthalpy is especially useful in chemistry, where pressure is nearly always constant but volume is not under control. Since chemistry (and biology) takes place at constant pressure and temperature, when a chemical reaction occurs the heat released is $Q = \Delta H$. Thus when you measure the heat released or absorbed in a reaction, you are measuring the enthalpy.

Consider for example the hydrogenation of ethene (C$_2$H$_4$) into ethane (C$_2$H$_6$):

$$\text{H}_2 + \text{C}_2\text{H}_4 \to \text{C}_2\text{H}_6$$
Interactive 3: Reaction Enthalpy Diagram

Enthalpy diagram for ethene hydrogenation showing how temperature affects spontaneity via the entropy penalty.

The standard enthalpy of reaction is $\Delta_r H^\circ = -136.3 \pm 0.3\;\text{kJ/mol}$. Since the enthalpy change is negative, the reaction is exothermicA reaction that releases heat ($\Delta H < 0$) (heat is released).

If we don't know the reaction enthalpy, we can compute it by taking the differences of the enthalpies of the products and reactants. That we can add and subtract enthalpies to get the reaction enthalpy is known as Hess's lawThe total enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the pathway, so we can add enthalpies of intermediate steps.

Computing Reaction Enthalpies
  • Using enthalpies of reaction is best (when available).
  • Using enthalpies of formation and Hess' law is the most convenient compromise.
  • Using bond enthalpies is a last resort, only recommended when formation enthalpies are not available.

8.4.2 Expansion Work

A natural question to ask is whether the difference between enthalpy and energy even matters. The difference is $\Delta(PV)$.

For chemical reactions in solids (e.g. aragonite converting to calcite), $P\Delta V \ll \Delta H$ and enthalpy and energy changes are nearly identical.

Volume change is more important when the total number of molecules is not the same on both sides of a reaction. For the formation of ammonia:

$$3\text{H}_2 + \text{N}_2 \to 2\text{NH}_3$$

This converts 4 molecules into 2, so $\Delta n = -2$. Using the ideal gas law:

$$\Delta(PV) = (\Delta n)RT = -2 \times 8.3\;\frac{\text{J}}{\text{mol}\cdot\text{K}} \times 298\;\text{K} = -4.9\;\frac{\text{kJ}}{\text{mol}}$$ (20)

Compared to $\Delta H = -97\;\text{kJ/mol}$, this is about 5%. As a rule of thumb, enthalpies and energies are pretty similar for solids and liquids, but for gases the difference can be relatively large. The difference is $\Delta H - \Delta E = (\Delta n)RT$.

8.5 Gibbs Free Energy

Gibbs free energy$G = H - TS = E + PV - TS$: the thermodynamic potential minimized at equilibrium at constant $T$ and $P$ is defined as

$$G \equiv H - TS = E + PV - TS$$ (21)

The differential of $G$ is

$$dG = VdP - SdT + \mu dN$$ (22)

so $G = G(P, N, T)$ and

$$\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial T}\right)_{P,N} = -S;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial P}\right)_{T,N} = V;\qquad \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial N}\right)_{P,T} = \mu$$ (23)

Gibbs free energy is the constant pressure version of Helmholtz free energy. At constant pressure and constant temperature, Gibbs free energy has its minimum value at equilibrium.

Direction of Reactions

In a chemical reaction at constant $T$ and $P$, the total entropy change is $\Delta S = \Delta_r S - \frac{\Delta_r H}{T} = -\frac{\Delta_r G}{T}$. Since total entropy always increases, the reaction can only proceed if $\Delta_r G < 0$. Thus the sign of the Gibbs free energy change indicates which way the reaction will spontaneously proceed.

For an ideal monatomic gas, using $V = Nk_BT/P$:

$$G = -Nk_BT\left(\ln\frac{k_BT}{P} + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{2\pi mk_BT}{h^2}\right)\right)$$ (24)

Then $\mu = G/N = k_BT\ln(n\lambda^3)$, confirming that for a monatomic ideal gas the chemical potential is the Gibbs free energy per molecule.

Note that we found $G = \mu N$ for the ideal gas. This relation is actually very general. It follows immediately from the Euler relation $E = TS - PV + \mu N$. When there are multiple species, $G = \sum \mu_i N_i$.

8.5.1 Partial Pressure

If we have a gas with $N_1$ molecules of type 1 and $N_2$ molecules of type 2, the partial pressure for type $i$ is

$$P_i \equiv \frac{N_i}{V}k_BT \qquad \text{(ideal gases)}$$ (25)

This gives Dalton's law$P_i/P = N_i/N$: the partial pressure of a gas in a mixture is proportional to its mole fraction:

$$\frac{P_i}{P} = \frac{N_i}{N}$$ (26)

The total Gibbs free energy is extensive: $G = G_1 + G_2 + \cdots$, where each $G_i$ uses the partial pressure $P_i$.

8.5.2 Law of Mass Action

Now we want to generalize to an arbitrary mixture of ideal gases. The partition function for a single molecule of an ideal gas is

$$\zeta = Z_{\text{single molecule}} = \int \frac{d^3x\,d^3p}{h^3}\sum_{\text{internal states } j} e^{-\beta\left(\frac{\vec{p}^2}{2m} + \epsilon_j\right)}$$ (27)

Including binding energy $\epsilon_b$ and a vibrational mode of frequency $\omega$:

$$\zeta = \frac{V}{\lambda^3}e^{-\beta\epsilon_b}\frac{1}{2\sinh\left(\frac{\beta}{2}\hbar\omega\right)}$$ (28)

For $N_1$ molecules, the partition function is $Z_1 = \frac{1}{N_1!}\zeta_1^{N_1}$. The chemical potential for gas 1 is

$$\mu_1 = -k_BT\ln\frac{\zeta_1}{N_1}$$ (29)

Using the notation $x_j \equiv N_j/N$ for the molar fraction of a reactant, for a reaction like $2A + 3B \rightleftharpoons 7C$:

$$K \equiv \frac{x_C^7}{x_A^2 x_B^3} = \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_r G^0}{k_BT}\right) = \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_r G}{RT}\right)$$ (30)

This is the law of mass actionThe equilibrium constant $K$ relates molar fractions (or partial pressures) of reactants and products to the Gibbs reaction energy. The powers on the left hand side are determined by the stoichiometric coefficients and $\Delta_r G^0$ is the change in Gibbs free energy per particle.

Example: Decomposition of Water at 5000 K

In the reaction $\text{H}_2\text{O(g)} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_2\text{(g)} + \frac{1}{2}\text{O}_2\text{(g)}$ at $T = 5000\;\text{K}$ the Gibbs reaction energy is $\Delta_r G = 118\;\text{kJ/mol}$. Starting with 1 mol of H$_2$O, it will decompose into $\xi$ moles of H$_2$ and $\xi/2$ moles of O$_2$ leaving $1 - \xi$ moles of H$_2$O. The total number of moles is $1 + \xi/2$. Solving:

$$\frac{x_{\text{H}_2} x_{\text{O}_2}^{1/2}}{x_{\text{H}_2\text{O}}} = \exp\left(-\frac{118\;\text{kJ/mol}}{8.3\;\text{J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)} \times 5000\;\text{K}}\right) = 0.058$$

Solving numerically gives $\xi = 0.17$. Thus at 5000 K, 17% of the water molecules will be decomposed. At room temperature $\Delta_r G = 228\;\text{kJ/mol}$ and $\xi = 10^{-27}$.

Generally $\Delta_r G$ is of order hundreds of kJ/mol while $RT = 2.5\;\text{kJ/mol}$ at room temperature. So the factor $\exp(-\Delta_r G/RT)$ is almost always either very very small or very very large. Thus:

Rule of Thumb

If we mix some chemicals, the sign of $\Delta_r G$ tells which way the reaction will go.

8.5.3 Direction of Chemical Reactions

The direction a reaction proceeds is determined by the sign of $\Delta_r G$. It is useful to compute $\Delta_r G$ via:

$$\Delta_r G = \Delta_r H - T\Delta_r S$$ (31)

Both $\Delta_r H$ and $\Delta_r S$ are generally weakly dependent on temperature, while $\Delta_r G$ depends strongly on $T$ because of the explicit factor of $T$.

Example: Lime Kiln

Limestone (CaCO$_3$) can be converted to lime (CaO) via: $\text{CaCO}_3 \rightleftharpoons \text{CaO} + \text{CO}_2$

$\Delta_r H = 178\;\text{kJ/mol}$, $\Delta_r S = 161\;\text{J/(K}\cdot\text{mol)}$.

At room temperature ($T = 298\;\text{K}$): $\Delta_r G = 178 - 298 \times 0.161 = 130\;\text{kJ/mol} > 0$. The reaction does not spontaneously occur.

At $T = 1500\;\text{K}$: $\Delta_r G = 178 - 1500 \times 0.161 = -64\;\text{kJ/mol} < 0$. The kiln allows the reaction to occur.

Heating does not just make the reaction go faster -- it changes the direction of the reaction.

When a system is in equilibrium at some temperature and we add more of one product, the system will adjust to restore equilibrium. This is an example of Le Chatelier's principleA system will work to restore equilibrium by counteracting any change.

8.6 Osmotic Pressure

Osmotic pressureThe pressure resulting from a concentration imbalance on two sides of a semi-permeable barrier. It is an entropic effect. is the pressure resulting from a concentration imbalance on the two sides of a semi-permeable barrier. For example, if you put a raisin in water, the higher sugar content of the raisin forces the water to be drawn into the raisin, swelling it up.

Interactive 4: Osmotic Equilibrium

A semipermeable membrane lets water through but blocks solute. Water flows until hydrostatic pressure balances osmotic pressure.

To compute the osmotic pressure, we use the entropy of mixing. For $N_w$ molecules of solvent and $N_s$ molecules of solute, in the limit $N_s \ll N_w$:

$$\Delta S_{\text{mix}} = k_BN_s\left(1 - \ln\frac{N_s}{N_w}\right)$$ (32)

The chemical potential of the solvent on the mixed side is

$$\mu_w^{\text{mixed}}(T, P) = \mu_w(T, P) - k_BT\frac{N_s}{N_w}$$ (33)

Setting the chemical potentials equal on both sides of the barrier and expanding leads to:

$$\Pi = k_BT\frac{N_s}{V}$$ (34)

This is known as van 't Hoff's formula$\Pi = k_BT N_s/V$: the osmotic pressure has the same form as the ideal gas law, even for liquids and solids, because dilute solutes behave ideally. Remarkably, this formula has the same form as the ideal gas law $P = k_BTN/V$. The key insight is that for dilute solutes, the $N_s$ dependence is always linear, approaching ideal extensive scaling behavior.

For multiple solute species, the osmotic pressure is a colligative propertyA property that depends only on the total concentration of solute particles, not their identity:

$$\Pi = k_BT\sum_s \frac{N_s}{V}$$ (35)
Example: Blood Osmolarity

The osmotic pressure in blood is 7.65 atm at 37$^\circ$C = 310 K. The net concentration of dissolved solvents is:

$$n_s = \frac{\Pi}{RT} = \frac{7.65\;\text{atm}}{0.08206\;\frac{\text{L}\cdot\text{atm}}{\text{mol}\cdot\text{K}} \times 310\;\text{K}} = 301\;\text{mmol/L}$$

Mixing a little more than 50 g of sugar (about 2 tablespoons) with a liter of water gives 277 mmol/L, below this threshold, and will not increase your blood pressure.

Osmotic pressure really is an entropic effect. It has physical observable consequences and makes quantitative predictions that can be tested experimentally.

8.7 Grand Free Energy

There is one more free energy, called the grand free energy $\Omega$. Recall that the grand canonical partition function is $\mathcal{Z}(V, T, \mu) = \sum_k e^{-\beta\epsilon_k + \beta\mu N_k}$. We define

$$\Omega = -k_BT\ln\mathcal{Z}$$ (36)

Thus

$$\Omega(T, V, \mu) = \langle E \rangle - TS - \mu\langle N \rangle$$ (37)

We can also write $\Omega(T, V, \mu) = F - \mu N$. Using $dE = TdS - PdV + \mu dN$:

$$d\Omega = -SdT - PdV - Nd\mu$$ (38)

An important property is that $\Omega$ is extensive and depends on only a single extensive variable $V$. Since $\frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial V}\big|_{\mu,T} = -P$, we have

$$\Omega = -PV$$ (39)

This is a useful relation, similar to $G = \mu N$ for the Gibbs free energy.

8.8 Summary

Chapter Summary

  • Helmholtz free energy $F = E - TS$ represents the energy available to do work at constant $T$ and $V$. It is minimized in equilibrium. $F = -k_BT\ln Z$.
  • Enthalpy $H = E + PV$ is useful at constant pressure. $C_P = (\partial H/\partial T)_P$. Heat released in reactions at constant $P$ equals $\Delta H$.
  • Gibbs free energy $G = H - TS$ is minimized at equilibrium at constant $T$ and $P$. $G = \mu N$ for a single species. The sign of $\Delta_r G$ determines which direction a reaction proceeds.
  • The Euler equation $E = TS - PV + \mu N$ and Gibbs-Duhem equation $SdT - VdP + Nd\mu = 0$ relate all thermodynamic variables.
  • The law of mass action determines equilibrium concentrations: $K = \exp(-\Delta_r G/RT)$.
  • Le Chatelier's principle: a system works to restore equilibrium by counteracting any change.
  • Osmotic pressure $\Pi = k_BT N_s/V$ (van 't Hoff's formula) is an entropic effect. It has the form of the ideal gas law because dilute solutes always behave ideally.
  • Energy minimization is really free energy minimization, which really is entropy maximization.

Q9. Rubber Band Thermodynamics

A rubber band can be modeled as a one-dimensional system where the fundamental relation is $dE = TdS + \mathcal{F}dL$, with $\mathcal{F}$ the tension and $L$ the length (analogous to $-PdV$ replaced by $\mathcal{F}dL$).

(a)

Define the appropriate Helmholtz free energy $F(T, L)$ for the rubber band and write its differential $dF$.

(b)

Derive the Maxwell relation that relates $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial L}\right)_T$ to a derivative of the tension $\mathcal{F}$.

(c)

Experimentally, rubber bands become stiffer when heated: $\left(\frac{\partial\mathcal{F}}{\partial T}\right)_L > 0$. Using your Maxwell relation, determine the sign of $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial L}\right)_T$. Explain physically why stretching a rubber band decreases its entropy.

(d)

Predict what happens to the temperature of a rubber band when it is suddenly (adiabatically) stretched. Verify your prediction by holding a thick rubber band against your lip and quickly stretching it.

Q10. Langmuir Adsorption

Consider a surface with $M$ identical adsorption sites, each of which can bind at most one gas molecule. The binding energy per molecule is $-\epsilon$ (with $\epsilon > 0$). The surface is in contact with an ideal gas at temperature $T$ and chemical potential $\mu$.

(a)

Each site is an independent system that can be empty (energy 0) or occupied (energy $-\epsilon$). Using the grand canonical ensemble, write the grand partition function $\mathcal{Z}_1$ for a single site.

(b)

Find the average occupancy $\langle n \rangle$ of a single site as a function of $\mu$, $\epsilon$, and $T$. Show that it can be written in the form $$\langle n \rangle = \frac{1}{e^{-\beta(\epsilon + \mu)} + 1}$$ which is a Fermi-Dirac distribution (because each site holds at most one particle).

(c)

For an ideal gas, $\mu = k_BT\ln(P\lambda^3/k_BT)$ where $\lambda$ is the thermal wavelength. Define $P_0 = (k_BT/\lambda^3)e^{-\beta\epsilon}$ and show that the fraction of occupied sites simplifies to $$\theta = \frac{P}{P + P_0}$$ This is the Langmuir isotherm.

(d)

Compute the grand free energy $\Omega$ for all $M$ sites, and verify that $\langle N \rangle = -\partial\Omega/\partial\mu$ reproduces your result from part (b).

Q11. Clausius-Clapeyron from Gibbs

At a first-order phase transition (e.g. boiling), two phases coexist along a curve $P(T)$ in the $T$-$P$ plane. Along this curve, the Gibbs free energies of the two phases are equal: $G_1(T,P) = G_2(T,P)$.

(a)

By taking the total differential of $G_1 = G_2$ along the coexistence curve, and using $dG = -SdT + VdP$ (at constant $N$), derive the Clausius-Clapeyron equation: $$\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{S_2 - S_1}{V_2 - V_1} = \frac{L}{T\Delta V}$$ where $L = T(S_2 - S_1)$ is the latent heat.

(b)

For the liquid-gas transition, $V_{\text{gas}} \gg V_{\text{liquid}}$, and the gas is approximately ideal. Show that the coexistence curve becomes $$P(T) = P_0\,\exp\left(-\frac{L}{k_BT}\right)$$ where $P_0$ is a constant, assuming $L$ is approximately independent of $T$.

(c)

The latent heat of vaporization of water is $L = 40.7\;\text{kJ/mol}$. If the boiling point at 1 atm is 373 K, estimate the boiling point at the top of Mount Everest where the pressure is about 0.34 atm.

Q12. Thermodynamic Potentials of a Magnetic System

For a magnetic system, the fundamental relation is $dE = TdS + BdM + \mu dN$, where $B$ is the external magnetic field and $M$ is the total magnetization (replacing the $-PdV$ term).

(a)

Construct the "magnetic Helmholtz free energy" $F(T, M, N) = E - TS$ and the "magnetic Gibbs free energy" $G(T, B, N) = E - TS - BM$. Write the differential of each and identify the natural variables.

(b)

From $G(T, B, N)$, derive a Maxwell relation that relates $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial B}\right)_{T,N}$ to a derivative of the magnetization.

(c)

For a paramagnet, increasing the field at constant $T$ increases the magnetization: $\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\right)_B < 0$ (spins become less aligned as $T$ rises). Use this to determine the sign of $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial B}\right)_T$. Explain physically why increasing $B$ at constant $T$ decreases the entropy.

(d)

In adiabatic demagnetization, a paramagnet is first magnetized at constant $T$ (in contact with a heat bath), then thermally isolated, and finally the field is slowly reduced to zero. Using your result from (c), explain why this process cools the paramagnet. This technique is used to reach temperatures below 1 mK.
