Chapter 4

Temperature

1. Introduction

In the last lecture, we considered an ideal gas that is completely specified by the positions $\vec{q}_i$ and momenta $\vec{p}_i$ of the $N$ molecules. We found the total number of states of the gas for large $N$ was

$$\Omega(N, V, E) = e^{\frac{3}{2}N}\left(\frac{V}{(\Delta q\,\Delta p)^3}\right)^N \left(\frac{4\pi m E}{3N}\right)^{\frac{3N}{2}}$$(1)

Then we asked how many fewer states were accessible if we know the velocity of a single molecule. This told us the probability that the molecule had that velocity, leading to the Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distributionThe probability distribution for molecular speeds in an ideal gas at thermal equilibrium. It was first derived by James Clerk Maxwell in 1860.

$$P(\vec{v}) = \left(\frac{3m}{4\pi\bar{\epsilon}}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{3}{4\bar{\epsilon}}m\vec{v}^{\,2}}, \qquad P(v) = 4\pi v^2 \left(\frac{3m}{4\pi\bar{\epsilon}}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{3mv^2}{4\bar{\epsilon}}}$$(2)

where $\bar{\epsilon} = \frac{E}{N}$.

The key to the computation of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution is that the number of states $\Omega(N, V, E)$ is an extremely rapidly varying function of energy, $\Omega \sim E^{3N/2}$. In this lecture we will see how to use the rapid variation of $\Omega$ to extract some general features of arbitrary systems. This will lead to the concept of temperature, as a constant among systems that can exchange energy.

2. Temperature

We defined $\Omega(E, V, N)$ for a system as the number of microstatesA microstate is a complete specification of every particle's position and momentum. The set of all microstates compatible with given macroscopic parameters defines $\Omega$. compatible with some macroscopic parameters. What happens if we have two different types of systems that can interact? For example, nitrogen and oxygen gas in the air around you. The gas molecules can collide with each other and exchange energy, but the two types of gas are still distinct. What can we say about how the energy of the system is distributed among the two gases in equilibrium?

Say the total energy of both gases combined is $E$. By energy conservation, $E$ does not change with time. So if there is energy $E_1$ in one gas, then the other gas has to have energy $E_2 = E - E_1$. Then the number of states with this partitioning is given by

$$\Omega(E, E_1) = \Omega_1(E_1)\,\Omega_2(E - E_1)$$(3)

where $\Omega_1(E)$ and $\Omega_2(E)$ are the number of microstates of the two gases separately. The functions $\Omega_1$ and $\Omega_2$ can be completely different — they don't have to be ideal gases. The probability of finding the system in a state with energy $E_1$ in the first gas is then

$$P(E_1) = C\,\Omega_1(E_1)\,\Omega_2(E - E_1)$$(4)

where $C$ is a normalization constant.

For example, let's say these are ideal monatomic gases where $\Omega \sim E^{\frac{3}{2}N}$. Then

$$P(E_1) = C' \times E_1^{\frac{3N_1}{2}}\,(E - E_1)^{\frac{3N_2}{2}}$$(5)

where $N_1$ and $N_2 = N - N_1$ are the numbers of the different types of gases and the constant $C'$ has absorbed any factors that don't depend on $E_1$.

This function looks like a Gaussian and has a very sharp peak around the most likely value $\langle E_1\rangle$. Using similar techniques to those we employed for the binomial distribution, we find

$$\langle E_1\rangle = \frac{N_1}{N_1 + N_2} E$$(6)

and the width of the distribution is

$$\sigma = \frac{\sqrt{N_1 N_2}}{(N_1 + N_2)^{3/2}} \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\; E$$(7)
Derivation: most probable $E_1$ and Gaussian width

Finding the most probable $E_1$. We have $P(E_1) = C' \, E_1^{3N_1/2}\,(E - E_1)^{3N_2/2}$. Taking the logarithm:

$$\ln P(E_1) = \text{const} + \tfrac{3N_1}{2}\ln E_1 + \tfrac{3N_2}{2}\ln(E - E_1)$$

Differentiating and setting equal to zero:

$$\frac{\partial \ln P}{\partial E_1} = \frac{3N_1}{2E_1} - \frac{3N_2}{2(E - E_1)} = 0$$

Solving gives $N_1(E - E_1) = N_2 E_1$, hence

$$\langle E_1 \rangle = \frac{N_1}{N_1 + N_2}\,E$$

Extracting the Gaussian width. Taylor-expand $\ln P(E_1)$ around the maximum $\langle E_1\rangle$. The first derivative vanishes there, so the leading term is the second derivative:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \ln P}{\partial E_1^2} = -\frac{3N_1}{2E_1^2} - \frac{3N_2}{2(E - E_1)^2}$$

Evaluating at $E_1 = \langle E_1\rangle = \frac{N_1}{N_1+N_2}E$ and $E - \langle E_1\rangle = \frac{N_2}{N_1+N_2}E$:

$$\frac{\partial^2 \ln P}{\partial E_1^2}\bigg|_{\langle E_1\rangle} = -\frac{3(N_1+N_2)^2}{2\,N_1\,E^2} - \frac{3(N_1+N_2)^2}{2\,N_2\,E^2} = -\frac{3(N_1+N_2)^3}{2\,N_1 N_2\,E^2}$$

So $\ln P \approx \ln P_{\max} - \frac{1}{2}\frac{(E_1 - \langle E_1\rangle)^2}{\sigma^2}$ with

$$\frac{1}{\sigma^2} = \frac{3(N_1+N_2)^3}{2\,N_1 N_2\,E^2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sigma = \frac{\sqrt{N_1 N_2}}{(N_1+N_2)^{3/2}}\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\;E$$

So that the relative fluctuationsThe ratio $\sigma/\langle E_1\rangle$ tells us how much the energy of subsystem 1 fluctuates compared to its mean. For large $N$, this is negligibly small. are

$$\frac{\sigma}{\langle E_1\rangle} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N_1}}\sqrt{\frac{2N_2}{3(N_1+N_2)}} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{N_1}}$$(8)

which goes to zero as $N_1 \to \infty$. So in the thermodynamic limit, the fluctuations around the most likely value are negligible and the energy distribution approaches a delta function.

More importantly, the most likely value can be found for any system, not just ideal gases. Starting from Eq. (4), the peak satisfies

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial E_1} = C\left[\frac{\partial \Omega_1}{\partial E_1}\,\Omega_2 + \Omega_1 \frac{\partial \Omega_2}{\partial E_1}\right] = 0$$(9)

which gives

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial E_1} = C\,\Omega_1(E_1)\,\Omega_2(E_2)\left[\frac{1}{\Omega_1(E_1)}\frac{\partial \Omega_1(E_1)}{\partial E_1} - \frac{1}{\Omega_2(E_2)}\frac{\partial \Omega_2(E_2)}{\partial E_2}\right]\bigg|_{E_1=\langle E_1\rangle} = 0$$(10)

Using that $E = E_1 + E_2$ is fixed, then $\frac{\partial}{\partial E_1} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial E_2}$ and so we can rewrite the second term. Setting $\frac{\partial P}{\partial E_1} = 0$ tells us at the peak, the following must be true:

$$\frac{\partial \ln \Omega_1}{\partial E_1} = \frac{\partial \ln \Omega_2}{\partial E_2}$$(11)

This equation says that the quantity $\frac{\partial \ln \Omega}{\partial E}$ must be the same in any two systems that can exchange energy and are in equilibrium. This naturally motivates us to define the entropyA measure of the number of microstates compatible with macroscopic parameters. Entropy is extensive (doubles when you double the system) and is maximized at equilibrium.:

$$S \equiv k_B \ln \Omega$$(12)

so that the equilibrium condition becomes

$$\frac{\partial S_1}{\partial E_1} = \frac{\partial S_2}{\partial E_2}$$(13)

This says that two systems in thermal equilibrium have the same value of $\frac{\partial S}{\partial E}$. We define the temperatureTemperature is defined as the inverse of the rate at which entropy changes with energy. Two systems that can exchange energy will reach the same temperature at equilibrium. through

$$\frac{1}{T} \equiv \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\bigg|_{V,N}$$(14)

so at equilibrium $T_1 = T_2$.

2.1 What is entropy?

At this point, we have defined the entropyEntropy $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ is the logarithm of the number of microstates, times Boltzmann's constant. It has units of J/K. as

$$S = k_B \ln \Omega$$(15)

and we have defined the temperature through $\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}$. Note that $S$ has the same units as Boltzmann's constant, $k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\;\text{J/K}$.

For a monatomic ideal gas, we already computed the number of states $\Omega$ in Eq. (1). Taking the logarithm and multiplying by $k_B$ gives the entropy as defined in Eq. (15):

$$S = Nk_B\left[\ln V + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{4\pi mE}{3N(\Delta p)^2}\right) + c\right]$$(16)

where $c = \frac{3}{2} - 3\ln(\Delta q)$ is a constant. Since $S$ should only depend on physics (not the arbitrary volume elements $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$), there must be a physical value for $\Delta q\,\Delta p$. This turns out to be Planck's constant, $\Delta q\,\Delta p = h$. Then

$$S = Nk_B\left[\ln V + \frac{3}{2}\ln\left(\frac{4\pi mE}{3Nh^2}\right) + c\right]$$(17)

Then, using $\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}$:

$$\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E} = Nk_B \frac{3}{2}\frac{1}{E} = \frac{3Nk_B}{2E}$$(18)

so that

$$E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$$(19)

This says the average energy of an ideal gas is $\frac{3}{2}k_BT$ per molecule. Since the energy is entirely kinetic, $E = \sum_{i}\frac{1}{2}m v_i^2$ with 3 velocity components per molecule, we also have $\frac{1}{2}m\langle v_x^2\rangle = \frac{1}{2}k_BT$, i.e. the energy per quadratic degree of freedom is $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$.

Derivation: $\langle p_x^2/(2m)\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}k_BT$ from the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution

The Maxwell-Boltzmann momentum distribution in 3D is

$$\frac{d^3P}{dp_x\,dp_y\,dp_z} = \left(\frac{1}{2\pi m k_BT}\right)^{3/2} \exp\!\left(-\frac{p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2}{2mk_BT}\right)$$

The average kinetic energy in the $x$-direction is

$$\left\langle\frac{p_x^2}{2m}\right\rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\!dp_x\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\!dp_y\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\!\!dp_z\;\frac{p_x^2}{2m}\left(\frac{1}{2\pi mk_BT}\right)^{3/2}e^{-\frac{p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2}{2mk_BT}}$$

Because the exponential factorizes, $e^{-(p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2)/2mk_BT} = e^{-p_x^2/2mk_BT}\,e^{-p_y^2/2mk_BT}\,e^{-p_z^2/2mk_BT}$, the $p_y$ and $p_z$ integrals are each just normalized Gaussians:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi mk_BT}}\,e^{-p_y^2/2mk_BT}\,dp_y = 1 \qquad\text{(same for } p_z\text{)}$$

So these integrals factor out and cancel, leaving only the $p_x$ integral:

$$\left\langle\frac{p_x^2}{2m}\right\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi mk_BT}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{p_x^2}{2m}\,e^{-p_x^2/2mk_BT}\,dp_x$$

Using the standard Gaussian moment $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x^2\,e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2}dx = \sigma^3\sqrt{2\pi}$ with $\sigma^2 = mk_BT$:

$$\left\langle\frac{p_x^2}{2m}\right\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi mk_BT}}\cdot\frac{1}{2m}\cdot(mk_BT)^{3/2}\sqrt{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2}k_BT$$

By the same argument, $\langle p_y^2/2m\rangle = \langle p_z^2/2m\rangle = \frac{1}{2}k_BT$. Adding all three gives $\langle \mathbf{p}^2/2m\rangle = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$, consistent with Eq. (19).

2.2 Temperature intuition

It is worth building a little intuition for the size of $k_BT$. At room temperature $k_BT = 25\;\text{meV}$. Thus the kinetic energy of any given molecule at room temperature is $38\;\text{meV}$. You can compare this to the energy scales of various physical processes:

Energy Scales at Room Temperature
  • $k_BT = 25\;\text{meV}$ at $T = 298\;\text{K}$ (room temperature)
  • Visible light: $1.5\text{-}3\;\text{eV}$ (much larger than $k_BT$)
  • Chemical bonds: $\sim 1\;\text{eV}$
  • Thermal energy per molecule: $\frac{3}{2}k_BT = 38\;\text{meV}$
  • Hydrogen bond: $\sim 0.2\;\text{eV}$

We also note the very useful conversion factors:

$$k_B = 8.617 \times 10^{-5}\;\frac{\text{eV}}{\text{K}}, \qquad \frac{k_B}{hc} = 0.69\;\frac{\text{cm}^{-1}}{\text{K}}$$(20)

Also, the Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution can be written in terms of temperature. Replacing $\bar{\epsilon} = \frac{E}{N} = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$ in Eq. (2):

$$P(\vec{v}) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_BT}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{mv^2}{2k_BT}}$$(21)

The most likely speed is $v_{\text{mp}} = \sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{m}}$, the mean speed is $\langle v \rangle = \sqrt{\frac{8k_BT}{\pi m}}$, and the root-mean-square speed is $v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m}}$.

3. Ideal Gas Law

One can also use the entropy to derive the pressure. We define

$$\frac{P}{T} \equiv \frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\bigg|_{E,N}$$(22)

For the ideal gas entropy from Eq. (17),

$$\frac{P}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial V} = \frac{Nk_B}{V}$$(23)

so that

$$PV = Nk_BT$$(24)

This is the ideal gas lawRelates pressure, volume, number of molecules, and temperature for an ideal gas. It holds for any dilute gas where intermolecular interactions are negligible.. We will re-derive this from a more physical perspective in the next lecture.

4. Equipartition Theorem

We showed that the average energy of a monatomic ideal gas is $E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$, i.e. $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ per degree of freedom. This result is actually much more general.

4.1 Non-relativistic particles

For $N$ non-relativistic particles in 3 dimensions, the energy is

$$E = \frac{p_1^2}{2m} + \frac{p_2^2}{2m} + \cdots + \frac{p_{3N}^2}{2m}$$(25)

Since each momentum component appears quadratically, and the $\Omega$ calculation treats all components symmetrically, each contributes $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ to the average energy.

Equipartition Theorem

Every quadratic degree of freedom in the Hamiltonian contributes $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ to the average energy at temperature $T$. For a term $\alpha q^2$ or $\alpha p^2$ in the energy:

$$\langle \alpha q^2 \rangle = \frac{1}{2}k_BT$$

4.2 Relativistic particles

It is not hard to repeat the calculation we did for the non-relativistic momentum for a situation in which the energy is linear. Energy for $N$ particles is

$$E = c(p_1 + p_2 + \cdots + p_N)$$(28)

where $p_j = |\vec{p}_j|$ are the magnitudes of each 3-momentum. Going through the same analysis, the number of states is

$$\Omega(N,V,E) \propto V^N\left(\frac{E}{N}\right)^{3N}$$(29)

which gives

$$S = Nk_B[\ln V + 3\ln E - 3\ln N + c']$$(30)

and therefore

$$\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E} = \frac{3Nk_B}{E}$$(31)

so $E = 3Nk_BT$, or $k_BT$ per degree of freedom. Each linear degree of freedom contributes $k_BT$ rather than $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$.

4.3 Universality of the Boltzmann factor

We can use this to derive a very general result. For a system where $\Omega \sim E^{fN/2}$ (with $f = 1$ for quadratic modes and $f = 2$ for linear modes, so that $E = \frac{f}{2}Nk_BT$), what is the probability of finding energy $\epsilon$ in a single mode? The probability is proportional to the number of states available to the remaining $N-1$ modes:

$$P(\epsilon) = \frac{\Omega_{N-1}(E - \epsilon)}{\Omega_N(E)} = \frac{(E - \epsilon)^{fN/2}}{E^{fN/2}} = \left(1 - \frac{\epsilon}{E}\right)^{fN/2} = \left(1 - \frac{\epsilon}{\frac{f}{2}Nk_BT}\right)^{fN/2}$$(35)

Now take $N \to \infty$, recovering an exponential:

$$P(\epsilon) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{\epsilon}{k_BT}\right)$$(36)

Note that the dependence on $f$ has dropped out: the probability of finding energy $\epsilon$ in a mode is independent of what type of mode it is — whether quadratic or linear, vibrational or rotational. No matter how the energy is stored, the probability of finding energy $\epsilon$ in any mode is $e^{-\epsilon/k_BT}$. This is a very powerful and very general result.

4.4 Summary

The point of the equipartition theoremEach independent quadratic degree of freedom contributes $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ to the average energy; each linear degree of freedom contributes $k_BT$. is that $k_BT$ characterizes the energy in each mode independent of the other modes. So $\frac{3}{2}k_BT$ is always the average kinetic energy of a non-relativistic molecule in 3D, and $3k_BT$ is always the average kinetic energy of a relativistic (massless) particle in 3D. Equipartition holds if the degrees of freedom are independent, meaning there are no terms in the energy of the form $p_1 p_2$ that couple different degrees of freedom, and the energy is either all quadratic or all linear.

5. Heat Capacity

5.1 Molecular degrees of freedom

For an ideal monatomic gas in 3D (e.g., helium or argon) the energy is entirely translational kinetic energy, giving $E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$. The heat capacity at constant volume$C_V = \frac{\partial E}{\partial T}\big|_V$ measures how much energy is needed to raise the temperature by one degree at constant volume. is

$$C_V = \frac{\partial E}{\partial T}\bigg|_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B$$(32)

For a diatomic gas, things are more interesting. Think of a diatomic molecule (like $\text{N}_2$ or $\text{O}_2$) as two point masses connected by a spring.

Interactive 4: Diatomic Molecule Modes

A diatomic molecule can translate, rotate, and vibrate. Each mode contributes independently to the energy.

Once we have approximated the molecule as pointlike atoms, we can consider how these atoms can be excited. The molecule can either be excited to rotate along one of 3 possible axes, or it can be excited to vibrate (stretch and compress) along the intermolecular axis. For rotation around the intermolecular axis, the moment of inertia is negligibly small (since the atoms are pointlike), so effectively there are only 2 rotational modes. For vibration along the intermolecular axis, there is 1 vibrational mode (which has both kinetic and potential energy, so it counts as 2 quadratic degrees of freedom).

Thus, the total energy and heat capacity depends on how many modes are excited:

Degrees of Freedom for Diatomic Molecules
  • Translation only (3 modes): $E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$, $C_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B$
  • Translation + rotation (5 modes): $E = \frac{5}{2}Nk_BT$, $C_V = \frac{5}{2}Nk_B$
  • Translation + rotation + vibration (7 modes): $E = \frac{7}{2}Nk_BT$, $C_V = \frac{7}{2}Nk_B$

5.2 Quantum freeze-out

In practice, the heat capacity of a diatomic gas like $\text{H}_2$ depends on temperature. At low temperatures, $C_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B$ (translation only). As the temperature rises, the rotational modes become excited and $C_V = \frac{5}{2}Nk_B$. At very high temperatures, vibrational modes kick in and $C_V = \frac{7}{2}Nk_B$.

Classically, rotational kinetic energy is $E = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$, with $I$ the moment of inertia and $\omega$ the angular velocity. In quantum mechanics, rotational energies are quantized:

$$\epsilon_{\text{rot}} = \frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\,\ell(\ell+1), \qquad \ell = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$(40)

and vibrational energies are also quantized:

$$\epsilon_{\text{vib}} = \hbar\omega_0\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$(41)

When $k_BT$ is much less than the spacing between levels, the mode is "frozen out" — it cannot be excited and does not contribute to the heat capacity. The mode becomes active when $k_BT$ is comparable to the energy spacing.

For the moment of inertia of a diatomic molecule, using the reduced massFor two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$, the reduced mass is $\mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}$. It characterizes the effective mass for the relative motion. $\mu$ and the bond length $r_0$:

$$I = \mu r_0^2, \qquad \mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}$$(42)

The rotational and vibrational energy scales are:

$$\epsilon_{\text{rot}} = \frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu r_0^2}, \qquad \epsilon_{\text{vib}} = \hbar\omega_0 = \hbar\sqrt{\frac{k}{\mu}}$$(43)

In spectroscopy these are characterized by wavenumbers $\tilde{\nu} = \frac{\epsilon}{hc}$:

$$\frac{k_B}{hc} = 0.69\;\frac{\text{cm}^{-1}}{\text{K}}$$(45)

Thus for $\text{H}_2$:

$$\epsilon_{\text{vib}} = hc\,\tilde{\nu}_{\text{vib}} = 0.54\;\text{eV}, \quad \frac{\epsilon_{\text{vib}}}{k_B} = 6300\;\text{K}$$(46)
$$\epsilon_{\text{rot}} = hc\,\tilde{\nu}_{\text{rot}} = 7.4\;\text{meV}, \quad \frac{\epsilon_{\text{rot}}}{k_B} = 85\;\text{K}$$(47)

5.2 Rule of thumb for gases

At room temperature, a good approximation for most molecules is that vibrational modes are frozen out but rotational ones are active. Monatomic molecules (like He) have no rotational modes, linear molecules (like H$_2$) have two, and non-linear molecules (like H$_2$O) have 3. Including the three translational kinetic degrees of freedom, the rule of thumb is

$$\frac{C_V}{Nk_B} = \frac{3}{2} \;\text{(monatomic)}, \qquad \frac{5}{2}\;\text{(diatomic/linear)}, \qquad 3\;\text{(nonlinear)}$$(49)

For air, which is mostly N$_2$ and O$_2$, the experimental value is $C_V/(Nk_B) = 2.63$, in good agreement with the prediction of $5/2$.

5.3 Solids and the Dulong-Petit law

What is the heat capacity of a solid? A simple model of a solid is a lattice of atoms connected by little springs (covalent bonds). If there are $N$ atoms, the contribution to the energy from the momentum of each atom is the usual $\frac{3}{2}k_BT$. The contribution from the position of each atom (vibrational potential energy from stretching the springs) is also $\frac{3}{2}k_BT$. Equivalently, there are vibrational modes in each of three directions, and each vibrational mode gives $k_BT$ (half potential and half kinetic). Either way, the total is

$$C_V = 3Nk_B$$(50a)

The molar heat capacityHeat capacity per mole of substance, $c = C_V/n$. A mole is $N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles. The ideal gas constant is $R = N_A k_B = 8.314\;\text{J/(mol·K)}$. (heat capacity per mole) is therefore $c = 3N_Ak_B = 3R$, where $R = N_Ak_B = 8.314\;\text{J/(mol·K)}$ is the ideal gas constant. This is the law of Dulong and Petit (1819): the molar heat capacity of many metals is roughly constant.

The metals all cluster around $3R = 24.9\;\text{J/(mol·K)}$, confirming Dulong-Petit. Water and ethanol have much higher molar heat capacities because each molecule has many internal degrees of freedom (rotations, vibrations) beyond simple lattice vibrations.

5.4 Specific heat

Heat capacity is an extensive quantity (proportional to the amount of material). An intensive quantity is the specific heatHeat capacity per unit mass. An intensive property that characterizes the material itself, independent of how much you have., which is the heat capacity per unit mass:

$$s \equiv \frac{\Delta E}{m\,\Delta T} = \frac{C_V}{m}$$(50)

Specific heat has units of $\frac{\text{J}}{\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}}$.

6. Principle of Maximum Entropy

In Section 2 we defined the entropy as $S = k_B \ln \Omega$. We showed that $\frac{\partial S}{\partial E} = \frac{1}{T}$ was the same among systems in thermal equilibrium. The formula $\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}$ tells us that the most likely energy distribution maximizes the total entropy. This is the principle of maximum entropyAt equilibrium, the macroscopic state of a system is the one that maximizes the total entropy, subject to constraints like fixed total energy..

The principle of maximum entropy can also be applied to determine the most likely distribution of particles among various groups. Suppose we have $N$ particles which are each in one of $m$ groups of size $n_i$. The total number of ways to arrange the particles is

$$\Omega = \frac{N!}{n_1!\,n_2!\cdots n_m!}$$(52)

This formula is due to Boltzmann. It is a generalization of the binomial distribution.

Now, if $N$ and the $n_i$ are all very large, then we can use Stirling's approximation:

$$\ln \Omega \approx N\ln N - N - \sum_{i=1}^{m}\left(n_i \ln n_i - n_i\right) = -\sum_{i=1}^{m} n_i \ln\frac{n_i}{N} = -N\sum_{i=1}^{m} f_i \ln f_i$$(53)

where $f_i = \frac{n_i}{N}$ are the fractions. Entropy is then

$$S = k_B \ln \Omega = -Nk_B\sum_{i=1}^{m} f_i \ln f_i$$(54)

This is important. We want to find what set of $\{f_i\}$ maximizes $S$. To maximize with the constraint $\sum f_i = 1$, we use a Lagrange multiplierA technique for finding extrema of a function subject to constraints. We add $\lambda$ times the constraint to the function we're optimizing. $\lambda$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial f_j}\left[-Nk_B \sum_i f_i \ln f_i + \lambda\left(\sum_i f_i - 1\right)\right] = 0$$(55)

which gives $-Nk_B(\ln f_j + 1) + \lambda = 0$, so $f_j = e^{\lambda/(Nk_B) - 1}$. Since this is the same for all $j$:

$$f_i = \frac{1}{m}$$(63)

We have found that, if we don't know anything about the system except for the total number of particles and the number of groupings $m$, then the probability distribution that maximizes the entropy is uniform: all groups are equally populated.

What if we know the average energy? Then we maximize $S$ subject to both $\sum f_i = 1$ and $\sum f_i \epsilon_i = \bar{\epsilon}$. Using two Lagrange multipliers:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial f_j}\left[-Nk_B\sum_i f_i \ln f_i + \lambda\left(\sum_i f_i - 1\right) + \beta\left(\sum_i f_i \epsilon_i - \bar{\epsilon}\right)\right] = 0$$(64)

This gives

$$f_i = \frac{1}{Z}e^{-\beta \epsilon_i}, \qquad Z = \sum_j e^{-\beta \epsilon_j}$$(66)

The variations around this probability are very small, scaling like $\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}$ by the law of large numbers. This is one of the most important equations in all of statistical mechanics, perhaps all of physics. It is the Boltzmann distributionThe probability of a system being in a state with energy $\epsilon_i$ at temperature $T$. The exponential suppression of high-energy states is the fundamental prediction of statistical mechanics.. The normalization factor $Z$ is called the partition function$Z = \sum_j e^{-\beta \epsilon_j}$ normalizes the Boltzmann distribution. It encodes all thermodynamic information about the system..

Note that the function we just maximized

$$H[P] = -\sum_i P_i \ln P_i$$(67)

is Boltzmann's $H$ function from Lecture 3. Boltzmann's $H$ theorem says that $H$ always increases with time. We'll come back to $H$, and see that it is directly proportional to entropy, when we discuss information theory in Lecture 6.

Chapter Summary

  • Temperature from entropy: Two systems exchanging energy reach equilibrium when $\frac{\partial \ln \Omega_1}{\partial E_1} = \frac{\partial \ln \Omega_2}{\partial E_2}$. We define entropy $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ and temperature $\frac{1}{T} = \frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\big|_{V,N}$.
  • Ideal gas: For a monatomic ideal gas, $E = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$ and $PV = Nk_BT$.
  • Equipartition theorem: Each quadratic degree of freedom contributes $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ to the average energy. Linear degrees of freedom contribute $k_BT$.
  • Universality of the Boltzmann factor: The probability of finding energy $\epsilon$ in any mode is $P(\epsilon) \propto e^{-\epsilon/k_BT}$, independent of the type of mode.
  • Heat capacity: For monatomic gas $C_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B$. For diatomic gas, $C_V$ ranges from $\frac{3}{2}Nk_B$ to $\frac{7}{2}Nk_B$ depending on which modes are thermally active. For solids, $C_V = 3Nk_B$ (Dulong-Petit law).
  • Quantum freeze-out: Modes are frozen out when $k_BT \ll \epsilon_{\text{spacing}}$. This explains why $C_V$ increases with temperature for diatomic molecules.
  • Maximum entropy principle: The equilibrium distribution maximizes entropy subject to constraints. With a fixed average energy, this gives the Boltzmann distribution $f_i \propto e^{-\beta \epsilon_i}$.
